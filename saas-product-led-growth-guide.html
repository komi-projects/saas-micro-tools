🌱 Product-Led Growth

Build products that sell themselves.

Why Product-Led Growth Wins

Product-Led Growth (PLG) is a go-to-market strategy where the product itself is the primary driver of customer acquisition, retention, and expansion. Instead of relying on sales teams to push the product, you design the product to pull users in — through viral loops, self-serve onboarding, and bottom-up adoption.

PLG companies grow faster and more efficiently. They have lower CAC because the product does the selling. They have higher retention because users adopt organically rather than being forced into contracts. And they have built-in expansion because the product naturally grows with the user's needs.

Slack, Notion, Figma, Zoom, Dropbox, and Calendly all used PLG to build billion-dollar businesses. Their products are so good that users bring them into their organizations — and the organization has no choice but to pay.

This guide covers how to design, measure, and scale a product-led growth engine.

PLG Pillars

1. End User Focus
Build for the end user, not the buyer. In PLG, the person who uses the product daily is the one who advocates for it. • Remove all friction for individual users. They should be able to sign up and start in seconds. • Make the free/trial experience compelling enough that users can't live without it • The buyer (manager, IT, finance) comes later, pushed by user demand • Don't gate core value behind a sales call. That's old-school enterprise, not PLG.
2. Viral Loops
Design features that naturally spread the product to new users. • Collaboration: Users invite teammates to collaborate (Slack, Notion, Figma) • Sharing: Users share content publicly with your branding (Calendly links, Typeform surveys) • Network effects: The product becomes more valuable as more people use it (Zoom, Dropbox) • Referrals: Incentivize users to invite others with credits or features (Dropbox's famous 500MB per referral) • Embeds: Users embed your product on their website (Intercom chat, Stripe checkout) • API integrations: Your product becomes part of their stack, hard to remove
3. Self-Serve Experience
Users should never need to talk to sales to get value. • Instant signup: No approval required, no sales demo needed • Self-serve onboarding: The product teaches itself through interactive tutorials • In-app purchasing: Upgrade with a credit card, no quote or invoice required • Usage-based pricing: Pay for what you use, scale up or down anytime • Help center: Comprehensive docs, videos, and community for self-support • Freemium: A genuinely useful free tier that demonstrates value
4. Time-to-Value (TTV)
Get users to their first "aha" moment in under 5 minutes. • Default templates: Pre-built setups so users see value immediately (Notion templates, Canva designs) • Import options: Let users bring their existing data/workflows (Slack import from email, Figma import from Sketch) • Quick wins: The first action should produce a visible, shareable result • Progressive complexity: Start simple, reveal advanced features as users grow • No setup required: The product works out of the box. Configuration is optional, not mandatory.
5. Bottom-Up Adoption
Individual users adopt first. The organization follows. • Land and expand: Start with 1 user, grow to a team, then the whole company • Team invites: Make it easy and valuable to invite colleagues • Admin controls: Add enterprise features (SSO, roles, compliance) after the team is hooked • Usage alerts: "Your team has grown to 15 members — upgrade to Team plan for advanced features" • Champion nurturing: Identify power users and give them tools to advocate internally
6. Data-Driven Iteration
Use product analytics to optimize the self-serve funnel. • Activation tracking: Measure which actions correlate with retention • Funnel analysis: Identify drop-off points in the signup-to-paid flow • A/B testing: Experiment with onboarding flows, pricing, and features • Cohort analysis: Compare user groups to identify what drives success • Feature usage: See which features drive engagement and which are ignored • Automated alerts: Notify sales/CS when a user shows enterprise signals (high usage, team growth, feature requests)

PLG Tactics

1. Freemium design:

• The free tier must be genuinely useful, not a crippled demo. Users should get real work done. • Limit by usage, not features. "5 projects free" is better than "no exports in free" • Include viral features in free: Collaboration, sharing, and invites spread the product • Show paid value, don't hide it: "You're using 4/5 projects. Upgrade for unlimited." • No time limits: Eternal free tier beats 14-day trial for PLG. Time pressure creates anxiety, not adoption. • Upgrade triggers: Usage limits, feature gates, and team size caps that naturally encourage upgrading

2. In-app monetization:

• Contextual upgrades: "You're approaching your limit. Upgrade to continue." (not intrusive, just helpful) • Feature teasers: "This feature is available on Pro. Try it free for 14 days." • Usage dashboards: "You've saved 10 hours this month with [Product]. Pro users save 25." • Team expansion prompts: "3 more team members want to join. Upgrade to add them." • Paywall design: Clear, simple upgrade flow with 1-click purchase. No quotes, no calls, no approval chains.

3. Growth loops:

• Invite rewards: "Get $20 credit for each friend who signs up and upgrades" • Team growth: "Your workspace is growing. Here's how to make the most of [Product] with your team." • Public sharing: "Share your [creation] publicly. It includes a 'Made with [Product]' badge." • Content marketing at scale: User-generated content (public templates, shared designs) drives SEO and awareness • Integration marketplace: "Connect [Product] with 50+ tools" — integrations make you stickier and drive partner traffic

4. Enterprise expansion:

• Product-assist sales: Sales engages only when users show enterprise signals (high usage, security needs, team size) • Self-serve to sales handoff: "Your usage suggests you might benefit from our Enterprise plan. Chat with our team." • Admin features: SSO, SCIM provisioning, audit logs, advanced roles — added when the organization adopts • Compliance: SOC 2, GDPR, HIPAA — enterprise requirements that don't affect individual users • Pricing transparency: Public pricing for self-serve. "Contact sales" for enterprise tiers only. • Land-and-expand playbook: Track team growth. Reach out when a company hits 10+ users.

PLG Metrics

Acquisition:

• Signup rate: % of website visitors who sign up • Viral coefficient (K): Average number of new users each existing user brings (K > 1 = viral growth) • Organic vs. paid signups: PLG should be 60%+ organic • Signup by channel: Which viral loops work best? • Time to signup: Minutes from first touch to account creation

Activation:

• Activation rate: % of signups who reach the "aha" moment • Time-to-value: Minutes from signup to first value experience • Onboarding completion: % who finish the guided setup • Feature adoption: % who try key features in first 7 days

Retention:

• Day 1 / 7 / 30 retention: % of users active after each milestone • Cohort curves: Retention by signup week • Power user rate: % of users who are highly engaged (daily/weekly active) • Resurrection rate: % of dormant users who re-engage

Monetization:

• Free-to-paid conversion: % of free users who upgrade (benchmark: 2-5% for freemium) • Average Revenue Per User (ARPU): Monthly revenue per user • Expansion revenue: % of revenue from upgrades, add-ons, and seat increases • Sales-assist conversion: % of product-qualified leads that close via sales • Payback period: Months to recover CAC (PLG benchmark: under 12 months)

North Star:

• Net Revenue Retention (NRR): >120% is excellent for PLG. Expansion from existing users drives growth. • Product-Qualified Leads (PQLs): Users who show enterprise signals based on product usage. • Natural Rate of Growth (NRG): Organic signups + organic upgrades + viral invites / total new revenue.

PLG Mistakes

1. Fake freemium: The free tier is useless. Users can't do anything meaningful without paying. That's a trial, not freemium. Freemium requires genuine utility.

2. Ignoring sales: PLG doesn't mean no sales. It means sales engages with qualified leads who already use the product. Product-assist sales is incredibly effective.

3. No viral mechanism: You call it PLG but there's no way for users to naturally spread the product. Every PLG product needs at least one viral loop.

4. Enterprise features too early: Building SSO, roles, and compliance before you have users. Focus on individual value first. Add enterprise features when you have enterprise demand.

5. Measuring vanity metrics: Counting signups instead of activations. A million signups with 5% activation is worse than 100K signups with 40% activation.

6. No expansion path: Users love the free product but there's no clear reason to upgrade. Define upgrade triggers and communicate them clearly.

7. Forcing sales contact: "Contact sales" to see pricing, upgrade, or get a feature. That's the opposite of PLG. Self-serve should handle 80% of revenue.

PLG Checklist

☐ Product delivers individual value without team adoption ☐ Signup takes under 60 seconds with no approval required ☐ First value moment achievable in under 5 minutes ☐ Free tier allows genuine utility (not just a demo) ☐ Viral loop designed and implemented (invites, sharing, collaboration) ☐ Self-serve upgrade flow with 1-click purchase ☐ Usage-based or transparent pricing visible without sales contact ☐ In-app upgrade prompts based on usage limits ☐ Product analytics tracking activation, retention, and conversion ☐ PQL (Product-Qualified Lead) scoring configured ☐ Sales-assist playbook for high-value accounts ☐ Team invite flow that encourages expansion ☐ Enterprise features (SSO, roles, compliance) ready but not pushed early ☐ Help center and community for self-support ☐ A/B testing framework for onboarding and pricing experiments ☐ Cohort analysis tracking retention by signup source and behavior ☐ North Star metric defined and tracked ☐ Monthly PLG metrics review with cross-functional team
