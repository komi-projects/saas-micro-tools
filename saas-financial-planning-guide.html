📊 Financial Planning

Forecast accurately. Manage cash. Make data-driven decisions.

Why Financial Planning Matters

Most SaaS startups fail not because the product is bad, but because they run out of money before finding product-market fit. Financial planning is the discipline that prevents this. It's not about spreadsheets — it's about knowing how much time you have, what levers you can pull, and when to pull them.

A good financial plan answers: How much runway do we have? What happens if growth is 50% slower? When do we need to raise? Can we afford to hire that engineer? What price point gets us to profitability? Without these answers, you're flying blind.

Building Your Financial Model

1. Revenue Forecast
Bottom-up: # new customers × ACV + # existing customers × expansion - churn. More accurate than top-down ("we'll capture 1% of a $10B market"). Segment by plan, channel, and geography. Model monthly for first year, quarterly for years 2-3.
2. Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
Hosting, payment processing, customer support, third-party API costs. SaaS COGS should be 15-25% of revenue. Track per-customer: if COGS grows faster than revenue, you have a unit economics problem.
3. Operating Expenses (OpEx)
• R&D (engineering, product, design): 40-60% of revenue in growth phase • Sales & Marketing: 30-50% of revenue (higher if PLG, lower if sales-led at scale) • G&A (admin, legal, finance, office): 10-20% of revenue Model headcount by role, then add 20-30% for benefits, taxes, and overhead.
4. Cash Flow
Revenue is not cash. Annual prepayments improve cash flow (get 12 months of cash on day 1). Monthly payments mean you're always 30 days behind. Model cash collections, not just recognized revenue. Track net burn: cash out - cash in.
5. Scenario Planning
Build three scenarios: • Base case: 70% probability. Expected growth, expected costs. • Best case: 15% probability. 2x growth, hiring accelerates. • Worst case: 15% probability. Growth stalls, costs must cut. Plan for worst case. Hope for best case. Execute base case. Update monthly with actuals.

Key Financial Metrics

• ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue): MRR × 12. The headline number for SaaS. • MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue): Sum of all monthly subscription revenue. Track new, expansion, contraction, and churn MRR separately. • Gross Margin: (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue. Target: > 75% for SaaS. • Net Revenue Retention (NRR): Revenue from existing customers including expansion. > 100% = net negative churn. • Burn Multiple: Net burn / Net New ARR. How efficiently you're growing. < 1.5x is excellent. • Runway: Cash / Monthly burn. Target: > 12 months. < 6 months = danger zone. • CAC Payback: Months to recover CAC from customer gross margin. < 12 months is healthy. • LTV:CAC Ratio: Lifetime value / Customer acquisition cost. > 3:1 is good. • Rule of 40: Growth rate % + Profit margin % ≥ 40. Balances growth and profitability.

Cash Management

Cash is king. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity, cash is reality.

• Annual prepayments: Offer 2 months free for annual payment. Improves cash flow by 10x (12 months vs. 1 month upfront). • Invoice timing: Bill on the 1st of the month, not anniversary. More predictable cash inflows. • Payment terms: Net 0 for credit cards (instant). Net 30 for enterprise invoicing (30-day delay). Factor this into cash flow. • Expense timing: Delay non-critical hires and purchases. Negotiate extended payment terms with vendors. • Emergency reserve: Keep 3-6 months of operating expenses in liquid savings. Not for yield — for survival. • Line of credit: Secure a credit line when you don't need it. It's insurance against cash flow gaps.

Fundraising Planning

When to raise: When you have 9-12 months of runway left. Not when you're down to 3 months — that's a desperation round with bad terms.

How much to raise: 18-24 months of runway. Enough to hit the next milestone (usually product-market fit or $1M ARR) plus a buffer.

Milestone-based planning: • Seed: $500K-$2M. 18 months to find product-market fit. • Series A: $2M-$10M. 18-24 months to scale go-to-market. • Series B: $10M-$30M. 24-36 months to become market leader. • Series C+: $30M+. Expand internationally, acquire competitors.

Alternative funding: • Revenue-based financing: Lighter Capital, Clearco. Repay from revenue, no equity dilution. • Venture debt: Debt for growth. Cheaper than equity if you can repay. • Angel investors: Smaller checks, faster decisions, more supportive. • Bootstrapping: No dilution, full control, but slower growth. Right for some businesses.

Financial Planning Tools

• Spreadsheets: Excel/Google Sheets. Flexible, free, everyone knows them. Good for first 2 years. • specialized SaaS FP&A: Pry, Jirav, Mosaic, Drivetrain. Better for multi-entity, complex scenarios, and team collaboration. • Accounting: QuickBooks, Xero, Wave. The source of truth for actuals. • Metrics dashboards: ChartMogul, Baremetrics, ProfitWell. Automated SaaS metrics from your billing system. • Cap table management: Carta, Pulley, Capbase. Track equity, dilution, and option pools.

Start with spreadsheets. Move to specialized tools when you have a finance team (usually post-Series A) or when spreadsheet complexity becomes unmanageable.

Board Reporting

Monthly board update (1 page):

• Headline: ARR, MRR, growth rate, burn, runway • Revenue: New, expansion, contraction, churn, NRR • Customers: Count, ACV, CAC, LTV, logo churn • Financial: P&L summary, cash flow, variance vs. plan • Product: Key launches, milestones, technical health • Team: Headcount, hiring plan, key departures • Risks: Top 3 risks and mitigations • Asks: What you need from the board (introductions, hiring help, strategic advice)

Be honest about misses. Boards hate surprises more than bad news. "We missed revenue by 20% because of X, here's the fix" is better than hiding it for 3 months.
