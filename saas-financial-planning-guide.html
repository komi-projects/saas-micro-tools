📊 Financial Planning

Build financial models and plan for sustainable growth.

Why Financial Planning Matters

SaaS financial planning is not spreadsheet exercise. It's the difference between building a sustainable business and burning cash until you die. Most SaaS startups fail not because the product is bad, but because they run out of money before reaching profitability.

Good financial planning helps you understand your runway, forecast revenue with confidence, make informed hiring decisions, and know exactly when (and if) you need to raise capital. It turns gut feelings into data-driven decisions.

This guide covers how to build financial models, forecast revenue, manage burn rate, and plan fundraising for sustainable SaaS growth.

Financial Model Components

1. Revenue Model
How money comes in. • MRR/ARR: Monthly/Annual Recurring Revenue from subscriptions • New revenue: New customer acquisition × ACV • Expansion revenue: Upgrades, add-ons, seat increases • Churn: Lost revenue from cancellations and downgrades • Net Revenue Retention: (Starting ARR + Expansion - Churn) / Starting ARR • Revenue recognition: When do you actually book the revenue? (important for cash vs. accrual)
2. Customer Acquisition
How customers arrive. • Lead volume: Marketing-qualified leads per month • Conversion funnel: Lead → Trial → Paid (with stage conversion rates) • CAC: Customer Acquisition Cost (sales + marketing spend / new customers) • CAC by channel: Organic, paid, referral, partner — which is most efficient? • Payback period: Months to recover CAC from customer revenue • Sales cycle: Average days from first touch to closed deal
3. Cost Structure
Where money goes. • COGS: Cost of Goods Sold (hosting, infrastructure, payment processing, customer support) • Gross margin: Revenue - COGS (benchmark: 75-85% for SaaS) • Operating expenses: R&D, Sales & Marketing, G&A (General & Administrative) • Burn rate: Monthly cash spent (gross burn = total spend, net burn = spend - revenue) • Headcount costs: Salaries, benefits, office, equipment (usually 60-70% of burn) • Variable vs. fixed: Which costs scale with revenue? Which are fixed?
4. Unit Economics
Per-customer profitability. • LTV: Lifetime Value (ARPU × gross margin × average customer lifetime) • CAC: Customer Acquisition Cost • LTV:CAC ratio: Benchmark 3:1 or higher • CAC payback: Months to recover acquisition cost (benchmark: <12 months) • Magic number: Net new ARR / prior quarter S&M spend (benchmark: >0.75) • Rule of 40: Growth rate + profit margin ≥ 40% (investor benchmark)
5. Cash Flow
When money moves. • Cash in: Customer payments (may differ from revenue recognition) • Cash out: Salaries, rent, vendor payments, taxes • Net cash flow: Cash in - cash out per month • Runway: Months of cash remaining at current burn rate • Working capital: Cash tied up in accounts receivable, prepayments, inventory • Annual prepay impact: Annual contracts paid upfront massively improve cash flow

Forecasting Methods

1. Bottom-up forecasting:

• Start with inputs: Leads, conversion rates, ACV, churn • Build the funnel: 1,000 leads → 200 trials (20%) → 40 customers (20%) × $1,000 ACV = $40K new MRR • Add expansion: 40 customers × 10% expansion rate × $500 = $2K expansion MRR • Subtract churn: 200 existing customers × 5% churn × $1,000 = $10K lost MRR • Net MRR: $40K + $2K - $10K = $32K net new MRR • Cohort-based: Forecast each signup cohort separately with its own retention curve

2. Top-down forecasting:

• Market sizing: Total Addressable Market (TAM) → Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM) → Target market • Market share: "We capture 1% of our target market = $X revenue" • Growth rates: "Industry grows 20% annually. We grow 2x industry rate = 40%." • Sanity check: Does bottom-up match top-down? If not, one is wrong.

3. Scenario planning:

• Best case: Everything goes right (120% of plan) • Base case: Most likely outcome (100% of plan) • Worst case: Things go poorly (80% of plan, or lower) • Sensitivity analysis: Which variables have the biggest impact? (Usually: churn and CAC) • Monte Carlo: Run 1,000 simulations with variable ranges to see probability distributions

4. Rolling forecasts:

• Update monthly: Don't set annual forecasts in stone. Adjust based on actual performance. • Variance analysis: "We forecasted $100K MRR. We hit $95K. Why?" Learn and adjust. • Leading indicators: Track metrics that predict future revenue (pipeline, trials, activation) • Lagging indicators: Actual revenue, churn, expansion — confirm what already happened

Burn Rate Management

1. Calculate runway:

• Gross burn: Total monthly expenses (salaries + marketing + infrastructure + overhead) • Net burn: Gross burn - monthly revenue • Runway: Cash in bank / net burn = months until broke • Example: $500K cash, $80K gross burn, $30K revenue = $50K net burn = 10 months runway • Target: 12-18 months runway minimum. 6 months = panic mode.

2. Reduce burn:

• Freeze hiring: The fastest way to reduce burn. Each engineer costs $15K+/month fully loaded. • Cut discretionary spend: Events, travel, swag, tools — trim non-essentials • Negotiate vendors: AWS, software subscriptions, office leases — everything is negotiable • Remote work: Eliminate office costs (can save $500-2K/employee/month) • Deferred compensation: Founders take minimal salary until revenue supports it • Contractor vs. employee: Contractors cost 30% less (no benefits, no equity)

3. Extend runway:

• Annual prepay: Offer 20-30% discount for annual payment upfront • Price increases: Implement value-based pricing that captures more revenue • Expansion focus: Shift from new customer acquisition to expansion (lower CAC) • Services revenue: Professional services, consulting, training — immediate cash • Partnerships: Co-marketing, resellers, integrations — revenue without headcount

Fundraising Planning

1. When to raise:

• Rule of thumb: Raise when you have 6-9 months of runway left • From strength: Raise when metrics are strong, not when desperate • milestones: "Raise after hitting $1M ARR" or "Raise after proving unit economics" • Market timing: Raise when investors are active (not during market crashes) • Alternative: Can you reach profitability without raising? If yes, consider bootstrapping.

2. How much to raise:

• 18-24 months runway: Enough to hit the next milestone without panic • Dilution math: How much equity are you willing to give up? (Seed: 15-25%, Series A: 20-30%) • Milestone-based: "We need $X to reach $Y ARR with Z customers" • Buffer: Add 20% to your calculation — everything takes longer and costs more • Example: "We burn $50K/month. We want 24 months runway. We need $1.2M + 20% buffer = $1.5M"

3. Valuation:

• Revenue multiples: Seed (5-10x ARR), Series A (10-15x ARR), Series B (15-25x ARR) — market dependent • Growth rate: Higher growth = higher multiple • Unit economics: LTV:CAC, payback period, gross margin — better metrics = better valuation • Market size: Larger TAM = higher potential = higher valuation • Competitive landscape: Are you the leader? A follower? A niche player? • Negotiation: Get multiple term sheets. Competition drives better terms.

4. Investor materials:

• Pitch deck: Problem, solution, market, traction, team, financials, ask • Financial model: 3-year forecast with assumptions clearly stated • Cap table: Current ownership, option pool, proposed round impact • Due diligence: Financials, contracts, IP, legal — have it ready before they ask • Data room: Organized, comprehensive, professional (Notion, Google Drive, or specialized tools)

Financial Planning Mistakes

1. Optimistic forecasting: Assuming everything goes right. "We'll grow 20% MoM forever." Reality: Growth slows, churn happens, markets shift. Use conservative assumptions.

2. Ignoring cash flow: Focusing on revenue recognition while ignoring when cash arrives. A $1M ARR business with annual prepay has very different cash needs than monthly billing.

3. No burn monitoring: Not tracking burn rate weekly. By the time you notice, you might have 3 months left. Watch cash like a hawk.

4. Hiring too fast: The #1 cause of premature death in SaaS. Each hire extends burn. Only hire when the pain is acute and the ROI is clear.

5. No scenario planning: One forecast, no contingencies. What if churn doubles? What if CAC increases 50%? What if growth stalls? Plan for bad times, not just good.

6. Raising too little: Raising just enough for 12 months, then spending 6 months fundraising. You're always fundraising instead of building. Raise for 18-24 months.

Financial Planning Checklist

☐ Revenue model built (MRR, expansion, churn, NRR) ☐ Customer acquisition model defined (leads, conversion, CAC) ☐ Cost structure documented (COGS, OpEx, headcount) ☐ Unit economics calculated (LTV, CAC, LTV:CAC, payback) ☐ Cash flow model created (inflows, outflows, timing) ☐ Runway calculated and monitored weekly ☐ Bottom-up forecast built with cohort assumptions ☐ Top-down market sizing completed ☐ Scenario planning (best/base/worst cases) ☐ Sensitivity analysis on key variables ☐ Rolling forecast process established (monthly updates) ☐ Variance analysis process (forecast vs. actual) ☐ Burn reduction plan documented ☐ Fundraising timeline defined (when, how much, milestones) ☐ Valuation expectations set with comparable analysis ☐ Investor materials prepared (deck, model, data room) ☐ Cap table current and scenario-modeled ☐ Financial review meeting scheduled monthly
