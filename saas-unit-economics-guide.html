📐 Unit Economics

Does your business model actually work? Here's the math.

What Are Unit Economics?

Unit economics answer the most important question in SaaS: does it make economic sense to acquire a customer? If you spend $1,000 to acquire a customer who generates $500 in lifetime value, you're burning cash. If they generate $5,000, you've got a scalable business.

Unit economics are the per-customer financials: how much it costs to get them, how much they're worth, and how long it takes to recover your investment. Healthy unit economics mean you can scale profitably. Unhealthy unit economics mean you'll run out of money before you reach scale.

The Core Metrics

CAC — Customer Acquisition Cost
Total sales and marketing spend in a period / Number of new customers acquired in that period.

Include: salaries (sales, marketing, SDRs), ad spend, tools, content production, events, commissions. Don't include: customer success (that's retention), R&D, G&A.

Example: $50K marketing + $30K sales = $80K total. 40 new customers. CAC = $2,000.
LTV — Lifetime Value
Average revenue per customer × Gross margin × Average customer lifetime.

Or: ARPU × Gross margin / Monthly churn rate.

Example: $100/month ARPU, 80% gross margin, 5% monthly churn. LTV = $100 × 0.80 / 0.05 = $1,600.

Use gross margin, not revenue. A customer paying $100/month with 60% COGS is worth less than one paying $80/month with 20% COGS.
LTV:CAC Ratio
LTV / CAC. The golden ratio.

• < 1:1 — Losing money on every customer. Fix this immediately. • 1:1 to 3:1 — Surviving but not thriving. Viable but hard to scale. • 3:1 to 5:1 — Healthy. Good for growth. • > 5:1 — Very efficient. But you might be under-investing in growth.

Target: 3:1 or higher.
CAC Payback Period
How many months to recover CAC from customer gross margin.

Formula: CAC / (ARPU × Gross margin)

Example: CAC = $2,000. ARPU = $100/month. Gross margin = 80%. Payback = $2,000 / ($100 × 0.80) = 25 months.

• < 12 months: Excellent. Cash flow positive quickly. • 12-18 months: Good. Manageable. • > 18 months: Risky. Need significant capital to bridge the gap.
Burn Multiple
Net burn / Net new ARR. How efficiently capital converts to growth.

• < 1x: World-class efficiency. Every dollar burned generates more than $1 of ARR. • 1-1.5x: Excellent. • 1.5-2x: Good. • > 3x: Inefficient. $3 burned for every $1 of ARR. Unsustainable.

David Sacks' metric. Replaced "growth at all costs" with "efficient growth."

Unit Economics by Business Model

B2B SaaS (Sales-led):

• CAC: $5,000-$50,000 (higher for enterprise) • LTV: $25,000-$500,000 • LTV:CAC: 5:1 to 10:1 • Payback: 12-24 months • Key: Sales efficiency, deal size, expansion revenue

B2B SaaS (Product-led):

• CAC: $200-$2,000 (lower, no sales team) • LTV: $1,000-$10,000 • LTV:CAC: 3:1 to 5:1 • Payback: 6-12 months • Key: Conversion rate, viral coefficient, self-service onboarding

B2C SaaS / Prosumer:

• CAC: $10-$100 • LTV: $50-$500 • LTV:CAC: 3:1 to 5:1 • Payback: 3-6 months • Key: Viral growth, low touch support, high volume

API/Platform:

• CAC: $500-$5,000 (developer evangelism) • LTV: $5,000-$50,000 (usage-based scaling) • LTV:CAC: 5:1 to 15:1 • Payback: 6-18 months • Key: Usage growth, developer adoption, integration stickiness

Improving Unit Economics

Reduce CAC:

• Improve conversion rates (better onboarding, clearer value prop) • Shift to lower-cost channels (organic SEO, referrals, product-led) • Reduce sales cycle (faster closes = lower sales cost) • Automate sales (self-service for SMB, sales-assisted for enterprise) • Improve lead quality (better targeting, not more volume)

Increase LTV:

• Reduce churn (better onboarding, customer success, product-market fit) • Increase expansion revenue (upsells, cross-sells, usage growth) • Raise prices (most underutilized lever) • Improve gross margins (optimize COGS, infrastructure costs) • Extend customer lifetime (annual contracts, multi-year deals)

Shorten payback:

• Annual prepayments (get 12 months of cash on day 1) • Reduce CAC (as above) • Increase ARPU (upsell at onboarding, minimum plans) • Improve gross margin (as above)

Unit Economics Traps

❌ Ignoring time: A 3:1 LTV:CAC with 36-month payback is worse than a 2.5:1 with 6-month payback if you're cash-constrained.

❌ Using revenue instead of gross margin: Revenue LTV of $10,000 with 50% COGS is actually $5,000. Always use gross margin.

❌ Assuming constant churn: Churn is usually higher in month 1, lower after year 1. Using average churn understates early risk.

❌ Not segmenting: Enterprise and SMB have wildly different unit economics. Blending them hides problems.

❌ Forgetting expansion: NRR > 100% means LTV grows over time. Static LTV calculations miss this.

❌ Optimizing the wrong metric: A 10:1 LTV:CAC with $10K CAC and $100K LTV sounds great, but if payback is 48 months, you might go broke scaling.
