💰 Unit Economics

Understand the metrics that determine SaaS profitability.

Why Unit Economics Matter

Unit economics answer the most important question in SaaS: Does each customer generate more revenue than it costs to acquire and serve? If yes, you can scale. If no, growth just burns cash faster.

A company with great unit economics can raise capital, scale marketing, and invest in product. A company with poor unit economics is a ticking time bomb — every new customer accelerates the burn. Understanding unit economics isn't just finance — it's survival.

This guide covers the essential unit economics metrics, how to calculate them, and what "good" looks like at different stages.

Core Unit Economics Metrics

1. Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)
Total cost to acquire one customer. • Formula: (Sales + Marketing spend) / New customers acquired • Include: Salaries, tools, ad spend, content, events, commissions • Time period: Use the same period for spend and customers (usually a month or quarter) • Blended vs. paid: Blended includes organic; paid is just paid channels • CAC by channel: Know which channels are efficient vs. expensive • Early-stage CAC is high and lumpy — trend over 6-12 months
2. Lifetime Value (LTV)
Predicted revenue from a customer over their lifetime. • Formula: ARPU × Gross Margin × Customer Lifetime • Customer Lifetime = 1 / Monthly Churn Rate • Simplified: LTV = ARPU × Gross Margin / Monthly Churn • Gross margin matters: High-margin products have higher LTV • Expansion revenue: Include expected upgrades and cross-sells • Segment LTV: Enterprise LTV is often 10x self-serve LTV
3. LTV/CAC Ratio
The ultimate unit economics health check. • Formula: LTV / CAC • < 1: You're losing money on every customer (unsustainable) • 1-3: Marginal — might work with great retention and low churn • 3-5: Healthy — room to invest in growth • 5+: Excellent — you can aggressively scale acquisition • Target: 3+ for sustainable SaaS growth • Higher is not always better: LTV/CAC of 10 might mean you're underinvesting in growth
4. Payback Period
Months to recover CAC from customer revenue. • Formula: CAC / (ARPU × Gross Margin) • < 6 months: Excellent — fast cash recovery • 6-12 months: Good — standard for SaaS • 12-18 months: Marginal — requires capital to fund growth • 18+ months: Risky — high chance of churn before payback • Target: 12 months or less • Cash flow impact: Longer payback = more capital required
5. Gross Margin
Revenue minus direct costs to serve customers. • Formula: (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue • COGS includes: Hosting, CDN, payment processing, support, infrastructure • Does not include: R&D, sales, marketing, G&A • SaaS target: 70-90% gross margin • Low margin: < 50% (harder to achieve profitability) • High margin: 85%+ (more cash for growth)

Additional Economics Metrics

1. Net Revenue Retention (NRR):

• Formula: Starting MRR + Expansion - Contraction - Churn • > 100%: Revenue grows from existing customers without new sales • 100-110%: Healthy expansion • 110-130%: Strong (top quartile) • 130%+: Exceptional (Snowflake, Datadog) • Expansion is the best growth — it's high margin and predictable

2. Magic Number:

• Formula: Net New ARR / Prior Quarter S&M Spend • > 1.5: Excellent — efficient growth • 0.75-1.5: Good — invest more in sales and marketing • 0.5-0.75: Marginal — improve before scaling • < 0.5: Unhealthy — fix unit economics first • Used by VCs to evaluate SaaS efficiency

3. Rule of 40:

• Formula: Growth Rate + Profit Margin ≥ 40% • Example: 30% growth + 15% margin = 45% (pass) • Example: 50% growth + (-20%) margin = 30% (fail) • Balances growth and profitability • Used by investors to value SaaS companies • Early-stage: Focus on growth; late-stage: Focus on profitability

4. Burn Multiple:

• Formula: Net Burn / Net New ARR • < 1: Efficient — less than $1 burned for each $1 ARR added • 1-1.5: Good — reasonable burn for growth • 1.5-2: Marginal — expensive growth • > 2: Inefficient — too much burn for too little growth • Trend: Track monthly to see if efficiency is improving

Unit Economics by Stage

Seed ($0-1M ARR):

• Focus: Product-market fit, not unit economics • CAC: Often undefined (founder-led sales) • LTV: Hard to predict with limited data • Churn: Likely high as you iterate on ICP • Goal: Find 10 customers who love you, then measure

Series A ($1-10M ARR):

• Focus: Prove repeatable acquisition and positive unit economics • LTV/CAC: Target 3+ with 12-month payback • Churn: Reduce to < 5% monthly for SMB, < 2% for enterprise • NRR: Target 100%+ (expansion offsetting churn) • Goal: Show that $1 of S&M spend returns $3+ in LTV

Series B+ ($10M+ ARR):

• Focus: Scale efficiently, improve margins, prove profitability path • LTV/CAC: 5+ with clear expansion mechanics • Payback: 6-12 months • NRR: 110%+ (net negative churn) • Rule of 40: Target 40%+ • Goal: Capital-efficient growth with clear path to profitability

Improving Unit Economics

1. Reduce CAC:

• Organic channels: SEO, content, referrals, viral loops • Conversion optimization: Better landing pages, onboarding, trials • Sales efficiency: Reduce sales cycle, increase win rate • Channel mix: Shift spend to lower-CAC channels • Product-led growth: Let product sell itself, reduce sales touch

2. Increase LTV:

• Reduce churn: Better onboarding, proactive support, product stickiness • Increase pricing: Value-based pricing, annual contracts, price increases • Expand revenue: Upsells, cross-sells, usage-based pricing • Extend lifetime: Annual contracts, multi-year deals, auto-renewal • Improve margin: Optimize infrastructure, reduce COGS

3. Shorten payback:

• Annual prepay: Offer 15-20% discount for annual payment • Trial optimization: Increase trial-to-paid conversion • Faster time-to-value: Reduce onboarding friction • Self-serve: Reduce sales touch for smaller deals • Usage-based: Revenue starts immediately, not after sales cycle

Unit Economics Mistakes

1. Ignoring gross margin: LTV calculations that don't include COGS. A $100/month product with 50% margin is very different from one with 85% margin.

2. Using revenue instead of margin: LTV should use contribution margin, not top-line revenue. What matters is profit, not revenue.

3. Overestimating lifetime: Assuming 5-year customer lifetimes when data shows 18 months. Use actual cohort data, not aspirations.

4. No cohort analysis: Averaging all customers together. Enterprise and SMB have wildly different unit economics. Segment and analyze separately.

5. Static analysis: Calculating unit economics once and never updating. These metrics change monthly as you scale, iterate, and compete.

6. Ignoring payback period: Focusing only on LTV/CAC without considering cash flow. A 3-year payback with high churn means you never recover CAC.

7. No expansion in LTV: Calculating LTV based on current ARR without expected upgrades. NRR > 100% dramatically changes the math.
