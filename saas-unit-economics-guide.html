📈 Unit Economics

Master the metrics that determine sustainability.

Why Unit Economics Matter

Unit economics answer the most important question in SaaS: does the business model work? If you spend $1,000 to acquire a customer who generates $500 in lifetime value, you have a fundamentally broken business. No amount of growth will fix it. You'll go bankrupt faster as you scale.

Conversely, if you spend $1,000 to acquire a customer who generates $5,000 in lifetime value, you have a money-printing machine. The only question is how fast you can pour money into customer acquisition.

Investors, board members, and smart founders obsess over unit economics. They're the foundation of every financial model, fundraising pitch, and strategic decision. This guide covers the essential metrics and how to calculate, benchmark, and improve them.

Core Unit Economics Metrics

1. Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)
The total cost to acquire one new customer.
Formula:
CAC = (Sales Spend + Marketing Spend) / New Customers Acquired
Example:
Monthly sales spend: $30K (salaries + commissions)
Monthly marketing spend: $20K (ads + content + events)
New customers acquired: 50
CAC = ($30K + $20K) / 50 = $1,000 per customer
• Include all costs: salaries, commissions, ads, tools, events, content production • Exclude: Customer success, support, and account management (those are retention costs) • Time period: Use the same period for spend and customers (usually monthly or quarterly) • Blended vs. paid: Blended CAC includes organic; paid CAC is only paid channels • Benchmark: Varies by market. SMB: $200-1,000. Mid-market: $1,000-10,000. Enterprise: $10,000-100,000+
2. Lifetime Value (LTV)
The total revenue a customer generates before churning.
Formula:
LTV = ARPU × Gross Margin × Average Customer Lifetime
or
LTV = ARPU × Gross Margin / Monthly Churn Rate
Example:
ARPU (Average Revenue Per User): $100/month
Gross Margin: 80%
Monthly Churn Rate: 5%
LTV = $100 × 0.80 / 0.05 = $1,600
• ARPU: Total MRR / total paying customers • Gross margin: (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue. SaaS benchmark: 75-85% • Customer lifetime: Average months a customer stays. 1 / monthly churn rate • Expansion: Include expansion revenue if significant. LTV with expansion = (ARPU + Expansion) × Margin / Churn • Cohort-based: Calculate LTV for each signup cohort for accuracy
3. LTV:CAC Ratio
The ultimate sustainability metric.
Formula:
LTV:CAC = LTV / CAC
Example:
LTV: $1,600
CAC: $1,000
LTV:CAC = $1,600 / $1,000 = 1.6:1
• Below 1:1: Losing money on every customer. Fix immediately. • 1:1 to 2:1: Surviving but not thriving. Improve unit economics before scaling. • 3:1: Healthy and sustainable. The SaaS standard. • 5:1+: Excellent. Could invest more in acquisition. • Danger zone: Some investors worry if LTV:CAC > 5:1 — you might be under-investing in growth
4. CAC Payback Period
How many months to recover acquisition cost.
Formula:
Payback = CAC / (ARPU × Gross Margin)
Example:
CAC: $1,000
ARPU: $100/month
Gross Margin: 80%
Payback = $1,000 / ($100 × 0.80) = 12.5 months
• Under 6 months: Excellent. Very capital efficient. • 6-12 months: Good. Standard for healthy SaaS. • 12-18 months: Acceptable for enterprise SaaS with high LTV. • Over 18 months: Concerning. Requires significant capital to fund growth. • Cash impact: Long payback = need more capital to scale. Short payback = self-funding growth.
5. Magic Number
How efficiently revenue is generated from sales and marketing spend.
Formula:
Magic Number = Net New ARR (quarter) / Prior Quarter S&M Spend
Example:
Q2 Net New ARR: $200K
Q1 S&M Spend: $300K
Magic Number = $200K / $300K = 0.67
• Below 0.5: Inefficient growth. Fix unit economics before spending more. • 0.5-0.75: Moderate efficiency. Proceed with caution. • 0.75-1.0: Good efficiency. Invest more in growth. • Above 1.0: Excellent. Pour fuel on the fire. • Context: Higher CAC, longer sales cycle products will naturally have lower magic numbers
6. Gross Margin
Profitability after direct costs.
Formula:
Gross Margin = (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue
Example:
Revenue: $100K
COGS: $15K
Gross Margin = ($100K - $15K) / $100K = 85%
• COGS for SaaS: Hosting, infrastructure, payment processing, customer support, third-party APIs • Excludes: R&D, sales, marketing, G&A (those are operating expenses) • Benchmark: 75-85% for SaaS. Below 70% is concerning. • Impact on LTV: Gross margin directly affects LTV calculation. Higher margin = higher LTV.

Advanced Metrics

1. Net Revenue Retention (NRR):

• NRR = (Starting MRR + Expansion - Contraction - Churn) / Starting MRR
• Above 100%: Revenue from existing customers is growing
• 110-130%: Excellent (Snowflake, Datadog, Slack territory)
• Below 100%: Losing more revenue than gaining — unsustainable
• Gross vs. Net: Gross only counts churn. Net includes expansion and contraction.

2. Cohort Analysis:

• Track each signup cohort separately
• Cohort 0: Signup month
• Cohort 1: Retention after 1 month, 3 months, 6 months, 12 months
• Compare cohorts: Are newer customers retaining better or worse?
• Identify patterns: Which acquisition channels produce the best cohorts?
• Predict LTV: Use cohort curves to estimate full lifetime value

3. Revenue Churn vs. Logo Churn:

• Logo churn: % of customers who cancel (easy to understand)
• Revenue churn: % of revenue lost to cancellations (what matters financially)
• Can have high logo churn but low revenue churn if losing small customers
• Or low logo churn but high revenue churn if losing big customers
• Track both. Revenue churn is the board-level metric.

4. Expansion Revenue:

• % of new revenue from existing customers vs. new customers
• Expansion rate: % of customers who upgrade annually
• Net dollar retention: The gold standard metric (includes expansion + churn)
• The best SaaS businesses generate 50%+ of new revenue from expansion

Improving Unit Economics

1. Reduce CAC:

• Focus on organic channels: SEO, content, referrals, product-led growth
• Improve conversion rates: Better landing pages, onboarding, trial experience
• Increase sales efficiency: Shorter sales cycles, higher win rates
• Retargeting: Convert website visitors who didn't sign up
• Partner channels: Resellers, integrations, co-marketing (lower CAC than direct)
• Product-led growth: Let the product sell itself (lowest CAC of all)

2. Increase LTV:

• Reduce churn: Better onboarding, proactive success, retention playbooks
• Drive expansion: Upsells, cross-sells, seat increases, usage growth
• Increase prices: Value-based pricing that captures more of the value you create
• Annual contracts: Longer commitments = longer lifetime + better cash flow
• Improve gross margin: Reduce hosting costs, automate support, optimize COGS

3. Accelerate payback:

• Annual prepay: Collect 12 months upfront instead of monthly
• Higher ACV: Sell to larger customers with bigger contracts
• Faster activation: Get customers to value faster so they stay longer
• Expansion at 90 days: Don't wait for annual renewal — expand early

Unit Economics Mistakes

1. Ignoring gross margin: Calculating LTV using revenue instead of gross profit. If you have 60% gross margin, your real LTV is 40% lower than revenue-based LTV.

2. Underestimating churn: Using the most recent month's churn rate instead of a 6-12 month average. Churn fluctuates. Use stable averages.

3. Blended CAC confusion: Reporting blended CAC (including organic) as if it's your paid CAC. Organic customers cost less, making blended look better than reality.

4. Static metrics: Calculating unit economics once and never updating. They change with every pricing change, product improvement, and market shift. Track monthly.

5. Vanity metrics: Focusing on LTV:CAC of 5:1 while ignoring that payback is 24 months. Both matter. A great ratio with slow payback is still cash-constrained.

6. No cohort tracking: Using averages instead of cohorts. Your earliest customers might have 90% retention while recent ones have 60%. Averages hide the trend.
