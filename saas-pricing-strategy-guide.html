💰 Pricing Strategy

Price for value. Test relentlessly. Maximize revenue.

Pricing Models

👤 Per-User (Seat-Based)
Simple, predictable, easy to understand. But caps expansion — customers limit seats to save money. Best for tools where everyone needs their own account (Slack, Notion, CRM).
📊 Usage-Based (Metered)
Pay for what you use. Aligns cost with value. Scales with customer success. Best for API tools, infrastructure, payment processing (Stripe, AWS, Twilio).
🏢 Tiered (Good-Better-Best)
3-4 plans with clear differentiation. Most common SaaS model. Starter for SMB, Pro for growth, Enterprise for big teams. 60-70% of customers choose the middle tier.
🎯 Freemium
Free tier + paid upgrades. Acquisition machine but expensive to support free users. Best when free users create value for paid users (network effects) or when marginal cost is near zero.
🤝 Value-Based (Outcome)
Price based on ROI delivered. "We save you $100K/year, so we charge $10K/year." Hard to implement but powerful. Best for high-ROI tools (cost savings, revenue generation).

Pricing Psychology

Anchoring: Show the most expensive plan first. "$199/mo" makes "$49/mo" look cheap. The middle tier becomes the "smart choice."

Charm pricing: $49 instead of $50. $99 instead of $100. The left-digit effect. $49 feels closer to $40 than $50.

Decoy effect: Add a plan that's clearly worse value. $49 (1 user), $99 (5 users), $129 (5 users + priority). The $99 plan looks like a steal.

Loss aversion: Frame annual billing as "Save 20%" not "Pay annually." People avoid losses more than they seek gains.

Social proof: "Most popular" badge on the middle tier. "Trusted by 10,000+ teams." People follow the crowd.

Willingness to Pay Research

Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter: Ask 4 questions:

1. At what price would you consider this too expensive? (Upper limit)
2. At what price would you consider this so cheap you'd question quality? (Lower limit)
3. At what price would you consider this a bargain? (Good value)
4. At what price would you consider this starting to get expensive? (Marginal)

Plot the cumulative distributions. The intersection of "too cheap" and "too expensive" = optimal price point.

Gabor-Granger: Show product, ask "Would you buy at $X?" Vary X. Plot demand curve. Find revenue-maximizing price (price × % willing to buy).

Pricing Tests to Run

1. Annual vs. Monthly: Offer 2 months free for annual. Test different discounts (15%, 20%, 25%).

2. Plan names: "Starter/Pro/Enterprise" vs. "Basic/Business/Enterprise" vs. "Free/Growth/Scale." Names affect perceived value.

3. Feature bundling: Which features drive upgrades? Test moving features between tiers.

4. Price increases: Test on new customers first. Grandfather existing customers. Monitor churn closely.

5. Add-ons: Test unbundling premium features as add-ons vs. including in higher tiers.

Pricing Metrics

• ARPU (Average Revenue Per User): Total MRR / total users. Should grow with pricing optimization.
• ACV (Average Contract Value): Average annual contract. Enterprise focus = higher ACV.
• Expansion revenue %: % of new MRR from existing customers. Target: 20-30%.
• Plan distribution: % on each tier. 60-70% on middle tier is healthy.
• Price sensitivity: Churn rate after price increase. <2% increase in churn is acceptable.
