💰 Pricing Strategy

Design pricing that maximizes revenue and aligns with value.

Why Pricing is Strategy

Pricing is the fastest way to increase revenue. A 1% improvement in pricing can increase profits by 11% (McKinsey). And unlike acquiring new customers, better pricing requires no additional marketing spend, no more sales hires, and no product changes.

But most SaaS companies underprice. They set prices based on cost plus margin, or by copying competitors, or by guessing. Then they fear raising prices will cause churn. The result: they leave 30-50% of potential revenue on the table.

This guide covers how to design pricing models, test willingness to pay, and implement price changes that increase revenue without increasing churn.

Pricing Models

1. Flat Rate (Simple)
One price, all features, unlimited usage. • Pros: Simple to understand, easy to sell, predictable revenue • Cons: No expansion revenue, one-size-fits-all, misses high-value customers • Best for: Simple tools with uniform value (Basecamp, Buffer early days) • Example: "$99/month for everything"
2. Per-User (Seat-Based)
Charge per user per month. • Pros: Scales with customer growth, predictable, easy to calculate • Cons: Creates friction to add users, encourages seat sharing, doesn't capture usage value • Best for: Tools where each user gets independent value (Slack, Notion, Zoom) • Variations: Per-user with tiers (Basic $10, Pro $20, Enterprise $30) • Watch out: "$50/user" sounds expensive at 100 users. Consider per-seat pricing that drops with volume.
3. Usage-Based (Consumption)
Charge for what customers actually use. • Pros: Aligns price with value, low barrier to entry, natural expansion • Cons: Unpredictable revenue, complex billing, customers fear runaway costs • Best for: Infrastructure, APIs, messaging, data processing (AWS, Twilio, Stripe) • Variations: Per API call, per GB stored, per transaction, per email sent • Hybrid: Base fee + usage overage to reduce unpredictability
4. Tiered (Good-Better-Best)
Multiple plans with increasing features and limits. • Pros: Captures different segments, clear upgrade path, price anchoring • Cons: Complex to design, can confuse customers, requires feature gating • Best for: Most SaaS products (the industry standard) • Structure: Starter (individuals), Professional (teams), Enterprise (organizations) • Psychology: The middle tier should be your target. Most customers choose it. • Example: Notion Free → Plus ($8) → Business ($15) → Enterprise (custom)
5. Freemium
Free tier with paid upgrades. • Pros: Low barrier to entry, viral growth, product-led acquisition • Cons: High support costs, low conversion rates, free users can overwhelm resources • Best for: Products with network effects or viral loops (Dropbox, Slack, Zoom) • Free tier design: Genuinely useful, not a crippled demo. Limit usage, not features. • Conversion target: 2-5% of free users upgrade (varies by product) • Danger: If free is too good, nobody pays. If free is too limited, nobody signs up.
6. Custom/Enterprise
Tailored pricing for large accounts. • Pros: Capture maximum value from big customers, handle complex requirements • Cons: Long sales cycles, custom contracts, higher CAC • Best for: $50K+ ACV deals with procurement teams and compliance needs • Process: Discovery → Proposal → Negotiation → Contract → Implementation • Pricing: "Contact sales" — opaque pricing allows value-based negotiation • Minimums: Set a floor (e.g., $20K/year) to ensure profitability

Pricing Psychology

1. Value-based pricing:

• Price based on customer value, not your costs. If you save them $10K/month, charging $500 is leaving money on the table. • Understand their economic buyer: What metric do they use to justify spend? (revenue, cost savings, efficiency) • Quantify ROI: "Our customers save 20 hours/month at $50/hour = $1,000/month in value. Our price is $199." • Segment by value: Different customers get different value. Charge accordingly. • Test willingness to pay: Survey, Van Westendorp, or conjoint analysis

2. Price anchoring:

• Show the most expensive plan first. Everything else looks reasonable by comparison. • Enterprise tier: Even if nobody buys it, it makes Professional look like a bargain. • Decoy pricing: Add a slightly worse option to make your target plan look better. • Annual vs. monthly: Annual at 20% discount anchors customers to longer commitments. • "Most popular" badge: Social proof that pushes customers to your preferred tier.

3. Framing:

• "$99/month" vs. "$1,188/year" — monthly feels cheaper even when annual is better value • "Less than $4/day" — daily framing makes expensive products feel affordable • "Save 20% with annual" — focus on savings, not the total commitment • "Start free, upgrade anytime" — reduces commitment anxiety • "No credit card required" — removes friction and fear

4. Payment terms:

• Monthly: Lower commitment, higher churn, better for SMB • Annual: 20-30% discount, better cash flow, lower churn, better for mid-market • Multi-year: Deep discounts (30-40%), guaranteed revenue, enterprise only • Upfront payment: Annual paid upfront improves cash flow massively • Net 30/60/90: Enterprise standard but delays cash collection • Auto-renewal: Default to auto-renew with opt-out (reduces churn)

Pricing Implementation

1. Pricing page design:

• Clear comparison: Show what's included in each tier. Don't make users guess. • Feature gating: Critical features in higher tiers (but not so many that free is useless) • Usage limits: Seats, storage, API calls — clearly displayed • CTA clarity: "Start free trial" vs. "Contact sales" — match the tier to the action • FAQ section: Address common pricing objections • Currency: Show local currency for international customers • Guarantee: "30-day money back" or "Cancel anytime" reduces risk

2. Price changes:

• Grandfathering: Existing customers keep old pricing (reduces churn risk) • Advance notice: 60-90 days before price increases take effect • Value justification: "We're adding [features] and improving [capabilities]. New pricing reflects this value." • Opt-out offers: "Lock in current pricing for another year by renewing now" • Segment rollout: Test price changes on new customers first • Monitor churn: Watch for churn spikes after price changes. Be ready to adjust.

3. Discounts:

• Never default to discounts. They're addictive and devalue your product. • If you must discount: Trade for something — annual commitment, case study, referral • Standardize: Create a discount matrix (10% for annual, 20% for multi-year, 30% for referrals) • Approval process: Require manager approval for discounts >10% • Track discount rates: If >20% of deals are discounted, your list price is wrong • Never discount to close bad-fit customers. They'll churn anyway.

Pricing Metrics

Revenue:

• Average Revenue Per User (ARPU): Total revenue / total users • Average Revenue Per Account (ARPA): Total revenue / total customers • MRR/ARR: Monthly/Annual Recurring Revenue • Revenue per plan: Which tier generates the most revenue? • Expansion revenue: $ from upgrades and add-ons

Conversion:

• Free-to-paid: % of free users who upgrade (benchmark: 2-5%) • Trial-to-paid: % of trial users who convert (benchmark: 15-25%) • Plan distribution: % of customers on each tier • Upgrade rate: % of customers who upgrade annually

Efficiency:

• CAC payback: Months to recover acquisition cost • LTV:CAC ratio: Lifetime value vs. acquisition cost (benchmark: 3:1+) • Gross margin: Revenue minus COGS (benchmark: 75-85% for SaaS) • Discount rate: % of revenue sold at a discount • Price sensitivity: How demand changes with price (price elasticity)

Pricing Mistakes

1. Underpricing: The most common mistake. Founders fear rejection so they price low. Result: unsustainable unit economics, inability to invest in product, and customers who don't value the product.

2. Copying competitors: Their pricing is based on their costs, their strategy, and their mistakes. Your value proposition is different. Price based on your value.

3. Too many tiers: Five plans with overlapping features confuses customers. Three tiers is the sweet spot. Four if you have a clear enterprise segment.

4. No annual option: Monthly-only pricing kills cash flow and increases churn. Always offer annual at a discount (15-30%).

5. Hidden fees: Surprise charges for setup, support, or overages. Transparency builds trust. Opaque pricing breeds resentment.

6. Never testing: Setting prices once and never changing them. Markets evolve, value perception shifts, competition changes. Test pricing annually.
