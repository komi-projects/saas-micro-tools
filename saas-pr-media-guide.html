📰 PR and Media

Earn press coverage that builds credibility.

Why PR for SaaS

Earned media (press coverage you don't pay for) is the highest-credibility marketing channel. When TechCrunch writes about your product, or a respected industry publication quotes your founder, it signals legitimacy that no ad can buy. PR builds brand awareness, attracts investors, drives SEO through backlinks, and generates inbound leads from readers who discover you in articles.

But PR is not about blasting press releases to random journalists. It's about building genuine relationships, understanding what journalists actually need, and providing value that makes their job easier. The best PR doesn't feel like PR — it feels like news.

This guide covers how to get press coverage for your SaaS, from story ideation to journalist outreach to measuring impact.

What Journalists Want

Journalists are not your marketing channel. They're professionals with deadlines, quotas, and audiences to serve. To get coverage, you must understand what they need:

• Newsworthiness: Is this actually news? A product update is not news. A $10M funding round is news. A unique dataset revealing industry trends is news. • Exclusivity: Can you offer an exclusive? "We're giving you the story first, 24 hours before anyone else." Exclusives get attention. • Data: Journalists love data. Original research, industry benchmarks, trend analysis. "We analyzed 1,000 SaaS pricing pages" is a story. • Human interest: The founder's journey, the customer's transformation, the team's diversity. Stories about people outperform stories about features. • Contrarian takes: "Why [industry assumption] is wrong, according to our data." Controversy drives clicks. But be ready to defend your position. • Timeliness: Tie your story to current events. "How SaaS companies are responding to [recent event]." • Access: Can you connect them to a customer for an interview? Can the CEO do a 30-minute call? Access is currency.

PR Story Types

1. Funding Announcements
The most common SaaS press story. But journalists are flooded with funding news. • Make it interesting: "We raised $5M to solve [specific problem], not just 'to grow'" • Include traction: "Profitable since month 6, serving 1,000+ customers" • Name-drop investors: "Backed by [known VC]" adds credibility • Include customer quote: Real user saying why they need this product • Timing: Announce when you have something else to announce (product launch, major milestone)
2. Product Launches
New features, major updates, or entirely new products. • Frame as problem-solving, not feature-listing • Include before/after: "Teams used to do X manually. Now they do Y automatically." • Customer proof: Beta user who saw measurable results • Demo access: Offer journalists hands-on access, not just screenshots • Video: 60-second demo video increases coverage rates
3. Data and Research Stories
Original data is the most reliable way to get coverage. • Industry reports: "State of SaaS Pricing 2024" based on your customer data • Trend analysis: "The rise of AI in customer support" with proprietary metrics • Benchmarks: "Average onboarding completion rate is 34%. Top quartile hits 67%." • Methodology: Be transparent about how data was collected and analyzed • Visuals: Charts, infographics, and data visualizations journalists can embed
4. Founder Stories
Personal narratives that humanize the brand. • Origin story: "I built this because I experienced [pain point] at [previous company]" • Pivot stories: "We started as X, failed, then became Y. Here's what we learned." • Diversity angles: "A first-time founder from [underrepresented group] raises $X" • Lessons learned: "5 mistakes I made scaling to $1M ARR" — contrarian and educational • Must be authentic: Manufactured founder stories are transparent and backfire
5. Industry Commentary
Position your founder as a thought leader on industry trends. • Op-eds: "Why the SaaS pricing model is broken" in industry publication • Reaction pieces: "What [recent event] means for SaaS founders" • Predictions: "5 trends that will reshape SaaS in 2025" • Expert commentary: Offer quotes on breaking news in your space • Requires genuine expertise: Don't comment on topics you don't understand deeply
6. Customer Wins
Case studies that showcase real results. • Named customer (with permission): "How [Customer] reduced churn 40%" • Specific metrics: "Saved $50K/year, reduced time by 20 hours/week" • Before/after narrative: Clear transformation story • Customer quote and photo: Humanizes the story • Often covered as "customer story" rather than "vendor press release"

Building Journalist Relationships

1. Research before reaching out:

• Read their recent articles. What do they cover? What's their angle? • Follow them on Twitter/X. Engage with their content authentically. • Check their byline history. Do they write about companies at your stage? • Note their preferred contact method. Some journalists hate cold email but respond on Twitter. • Use tools like Muck Rack, Cision, or even LinkedIn to find contact info.

2. The first outreach:

• Subject line: Specific and newsworthy. "Exclusive: SaaS startup reaches $10M ARR without VC funding" • First sentence: Why them specifically. "I read your piece on bootstrapped SaaS and thought this fits your angle." • Pitch length: 3-4 short paragraphs max. Journalists skim. • Include: What's the story? Why now? What's exclusive? What's the proof? • Offer: "Happy to send more details, arrange an interview, or provide a demo." • No attachments: Paste everything in the email. Attachments get filtered. • Timing: Tuesday-Thursday, 9-11 AM in their timezone. Avoid Monday (inbox overflow) and Friday (weekend wind-down).

3. Follow-up:

• One follow-up, 3-5 days later. "Following up on my note from Tuesday about [topic]. Still interested?" • If no response after follow-up, move on. Don't harass. • Keep the relationship warm even if they pass. "No worries — I'll keep you in mind for future stories." • When they do cover you, thank them personally. Share the article. Tag them.

4. Become a source:>

• Respond to HARO (Help A Reporter Out) queries daily. Be helpful even if it's not about you. • Offer expert commentary on industry trends, not just your product. • Build a reputation as someone who responds quickly with valuable insights. • Journalists return to sources who delivered before. Become their go-to for your niche.

PR Tools and Distribution

Press release distribution:

• PR Newswire: Traditional wire service. Expensive ($500-$2,000) but reaches major outlets. • Business Wire: Similar to PR Newswire. Good for public companies. • Presspage: Modern press room hosting. Good for investor relations and media kits. • Notion / Google Sites: Free press kit pages. Include logo, founder photos, product screenshots, boilerplate. • DIY outreach: Most effective for early-stage. Personal emails to 10 targeted journalists beat mass distribution to 1,000.

Media monitoring:

• Google Alerts: Free. Monitor your brand, competitors, and industry keywords. • Mention: Real-time monitoring across web and social. Affordable. • Meltwater: Enterprise media monitoring and analytics. Expensive but comprehensive. • Brand24: Social listening and media monitoring. Mid-market pricing.

Press kit essentials:

• Company boilerplate: 100-word description of who you are and what you do • Founder bios: Photos, backgrounds, quotable expertise areas • Product screenshots: High-resolution, with and without UI chrome • Logo files: PNG, SVG, black, white, and color versions • Previous press coverage: Links to articles (social proof for journalists) • Contact info: Who handles press inquiries (founder, PR person, or agency) • Data and research: Any proprietary reports or datasets you're willing to share

Measuring PR Impact

Reach and awareness:

• Number of articles/placements • Publication reach (monthly unique visitors) • Social shares of coverage • Brand mention volume (before vs. after campaign) • Share of voice vs. competitors

Website and SEO:

• Referral traffic from press articles (check Google Analytics referral sources) • Backlinks acquired (check Ahrefs, Moz, or SEMrush) • Domain authority improvement over time • Organic search traffic for brand terms (people searching for you after reading about you) • Direct traffic spike (people typing your URL directly after reading an article)

Business impact:

• Trial signups attributed to PR (UTM parameters on press links) • Lead quality from press sources (do they convert at higher rates?) • Investor interest (did coverage attract investor outreach?) • Partnership inbound (did coverage attract partnership interest?) • Recruiting impact (did coverage improve candidate quality or volume?) • Sales cycle: Do prospects who mention press coverage close faster?

Important: PR is a lagging indicator. An article today might drive a signup 3 months later when the reader finally needs your solution. Measure long-term, not just immediate traffic spikes.

PR Mistakes

1. Spray and pray: Blasting the same press release to 500 journalists. Zero personalization. Zero research. Zero response rate. Target 10 journalists who actually cover your space.

2. Overhyping: "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "disruptive." Journalists have seen these words 1,000 times. They mean nothing. Use specific, provable claims instead.

3. Ignoring timing: Announcing on Friday afternoon. Announcing during a major news event. Announcing when the journalist is on vacation. Timing matters. Check the news cycle.

4. No follow-through: A journalist expresses interest, then you take 3 days to send materials. By then, they've moved on. Be ready with everything before you pitch.

5. Treating PR as a one-time event: "We got one TechCrunch article, we're done." PR is a relationship game. Consistent, smaller coverage beats one big hit and silence.

6. No crisis plan: Something goes wrong. A data breach. A founder says something stupid on Twitter. No plan = panic response = worse coverage. Have a crisis communications plan before you need it.

PR Checklist

☐ Story identified and newsworthy angle confirmed ☐ Target journalists researched (10-20 specific names and outlets) ☐ Press kit prepared (boilerplate, bios, screenshots, logos) ☐ Exclusive offer ready (if applicable) ☐ Pitch email drafted (3-4 paragraphs, no attachments) ☐ Founder available for interviews (calendar cleared for 48 hours post-pitch) ☐ Customer quotes and permissions secured ☐ Demo access or trial accounts ready for journalist testing ☐ UTM parameters on all links in press materials ☐ Social media plan for amplifying coverage ☐ Internal team briefed (support, sales, execs know what's coming) ☐ Crisis response plan documented ☐ Media monitoring set up ☐ Metrics dashboard ready to track impact ☐ Follow-up strategy planned (how to nurture journalist relationships long-term)
