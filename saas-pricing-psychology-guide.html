The 7 Pricing Principles

1. Anchoring
The first price seen becomes the reference point. Show the most expensive plan first (Enterprise $499), then your target plan looks cheap (Pro $99).
2. Decoy Effect
Add a third option that's slightly worse than your target. Example: Basic $29, Pro $49, Pro+ $54. Pro+ makes Pro look like a steal. Economist Magazine used this to increase Pro sales by 43%.
3. Charm Pricing
$99 feels significantly cheaper than $100. $49 vs $50. The left-digit effect: consumers focus on the first number. Use .99 for B2C, round numbers for enterprise.
4. Price-Quality Heuristic
Higher prices signal higher quality. A $99/mo product is perceived as more reliable than a $9/mo product. Don't race to the bottom — it hurts perceived value.
5. Loss Aversion
People fear losing what they have more than gaining something new. Frame trials as "You have 14 days of Pro features. Don't lose them." vs "Upgrade to Pro."
6. Weber's Law
The just-noticeable difference is proportional to the original stimulus. A $10 increase on $50 is huge (20%). A $10 increase on $500 is nothing (2%). Raise prices on enterprise plans more aggressively.
7. Choice Architecture
3 options max. Too many = decision paralysis. Highlight one as "Most Popular" (social proof + default effect). Make the CTA button for the popular plan more prominent.

Pricing Page Example

Bad ❌
Starter: $9/mo
Pro: $49/mo ← "Most Popular" (hidden at bottom)
Enterprise: $199/mo

❌ No annual discount shown
❌ No feature comparison
❌ "Contact us" for Enterprise (friction)
Good ✅
Enterprise: $499/mo ← Anchors high
Pro: $99/mo ← "Most Popular" badge + highlighted card
Starter: $29/mo ← Decoy makes Pro look better

✅ "Save 20% with annual" toggle (default to annual)
✅ Feature checkmarks with tooltips
✅ "Start free trial" CTA (no credit card)

Annual vs Monthly Framing

• Monthly: $99/month (lower barrier, higher churn)
• Annual: $950/year — "Save $238" (loss aversion)
• Best: Default to annual, show monthly as "or pay monthly"

Pro tip: Show the annual total prominently: "$950/year" not "$79/month billed annually. The yearly number feels like a commitment, which increases retention.
