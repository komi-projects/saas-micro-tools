🚀 Growth Hacking

Unconventional tactics for rapid growth.

What is Growth Hacking?

Growth hacking is the intersection of marketing, product, and engineering. It's about finding creative, scalable, and often unconventional ways to grow a business. Traditional marketers focus on brand and awareness. Growth hackers focus on metrics, experimentation, and rapid iteration.

The best growth hacks leverage the product itself to acquire users. They're often free or low-cost, scalable, and measurable. Dropbox's referral program, Hotmail's email signature, and Airbnb's Craigslist integration are legendary examples.

This guide covers proven growth hacking tactics specifically for SaaS businesses.

Acquisition Hacks

1. Product-Led Acquisition
Let your product bring in new users. • Viral loops: Users invite others to collaborate (Slack, Notion, Figma) • Sharing: Every piece of content includes your branding (Typeform surveys, Calendly links) • Embeds: Users embed your product on their site (Intercom, Stripe) • API integrations: Become part of your users' stack • Free tools: Build free calculators, templates, or utilities that drive traffic • Example: HubSpot's free CRM brings in millions of leads for their paid products
2. Content Engine
Create content that ranks and converts. • SEO-first: Target high-intent keywords with product-aware content • Comparison content: "Best [category] tools" — include yourself honestly • Templates and resources: Free downloads that require email signup • Original research: Survey your industry, publish findings (gets citations and links) • Video: YouTube tutorials, product demos, founder stories • Podcast: Interview customers, partners, and industry experts • Repurpose: One blog post → Twitter thread → LinkedIn post → Newsletter → Video
3. Community-Led Growth
Build a community that markets for you. • User communities: Slack, Discord, or Circle groups for power users • Industry communities: Host events, AMAs, and discussions • Expert network: Identify and nurture industry influencers • User-generated content: Encourage customers to share tips, templates, and use cases • Champions program: Reward top users with early access, swag, and recognition • Example: Notion's template gallery and community drive massive organic growth
4. Partnership Hacks
Leverage other companies' audiences. • Integration partnerships: "Works with Slack" drives traffic from Slack's marketplace • Co-marketing: Joint webinars, ebooks, and content with complementary tools • Affiliate programs: Pay partners for referred customers (15-30% commission) • Reseller programs: Let agencies sell and implement your product • API ecosystem: Developers build on your platform, bringing their users • Example: Zapier's integration marketplace is a massive acquisition channel
5. Engineering as Marketing
Build free tools that solve problems. • Calculators: ROI calculators, pricing estimators, cost savings tools • Generators: Invoice generators, contract templates, email signature creators • Checkers: SEO analyzers, website speed testers, security scanners • Comparators: Feature comparisons, pricing comparisons, vendor evaluations • Resources: Industry reports, salary guides, benchmark databases • Example: Shopify's logo maker and business name generator bring in entrepreneurs
6. Marketplace and Platform Hacks
Be where your customers already are. • App stores: Shopify App Store, Salesforce AppExchange, WordPress plugin directory • Review sites: G2, Capterra, TrustRadius — optimize profiles and drive reviews • Product Hunt: Launch strategically, engage the community, build momentum • Reddit: Provide genuine value in relevant subreddits (not spam) • Hacker News: Show HN with genuinely interesting projects or data • Indie Hackers: Share transparently, build in public, help others

Retention and Engagement Hacks

1. Behavioral emails:

• Trigger emails based on actions (or inaction) • "You haven't logged in for 3 days — here's what you're missing" • "You tried feature X but not Y — here's how Y helps" • "Your trial expires tomorrow — upgrade to keep access" • "Your team hit a milestone — celebrate!" • Timing matters: Send when users are most likely to engage

2. Gamification:

• Progress bars: "Your profile is 60% complete" • Milestones: "🎉 You sent your 100th email!" • Streaks: "You've used [Product] 7 days in a row" • Leaderboards: "You're in the top 10% of active users" • Badges: "Power User," "Early Adopter," "Community Champion" • Rewards: Unlock features, discounts, or swag for achievements

3. In-app engagement:

• Tooltips: Contextual tips when users hover or click • Modals: Milestone celebrations and feature announcements • Checklists: "Complete these 3 steps to get started" • Empty states: Beautiful, helpful screens when there's no data • What's new: Highlight new features without interrupting workflow • Nudges: "Invite your team — collaboration is 3x more powerful"

4. Re-engagement campaigns:

• Dormant user sequence: "We miss you" → "Here's what's new" → "Special offer" • Win-back offers: "Come back and get 30% off your first month" • Product updates: "Since you left, we added [feature you requested]" • Personal outreach: Founder email to churned users • Retargeting: Show ads to former users with new features or offers

Revenue Hacks

1. Expansion triggers:

• Usage alerts: "You're at 85% of your plan limit" • Team growth prompts: "3 team members want to join — upgrade to add them" • Feature teasers: "This feature is available on Pro — try it free for 14 days" • Success milestones: "You processed 10K records! Pro users process 100K." • ROI reports: "You saved $5K this month — Pro saves even more"

2. Pricing psychology:

• Decoy pricing: Add an expensive plan to make the middle tier look reasonable • Annual anchoring: Show monthly as "$99/mo" but annual as "$79/mo (save $240)" • Trial extensions: "Need more time? Complete this task for 7 extra days" • Bundle deals: "Buy annually and get onboarding services free" • Urgency: "Price increases next week — lock in now" (only if true)

3. Payment optimization:

• Annual prepay: Offer 20-30% discount for annual payment • Payment methods: Accept credit card, PayPal, ACH, wire transfer • Auto-renewal: Default to auto-renew with easy cancellation • Dunning: Retry failed payments 3+ times with email reminders • Grace periods: "Your payment failed — you have 7 days to update"

Growth Hacking Metrics

North Star Metric:

• The one metric that best captures core value delivery • Examples: Slack (sent messages), Airbnb (nights booked), Dropbox (files shared) • It should: Reflect value to users, be actionable, and predict revenue • Everyone on the team should know it and optimize for it

Growth rate:

• MoM MRR growth: % increase month-over-month • YoY ARR growth: % increase year-over-year • User growth: New users per month / total users • Viral coefficient (K): Average invites per user × conversion rate

Experiment velocity:

• Tests per month: How many experiments are you running? • Win rate: % of tests that produce positive results • Impact per test: Average lift from winning experiments • Time per test: Days from hypothesis to conclusion

Growth Hacking Mistakes

1. Growth without retention: Acquiring 1,000 users who all churn in a week. Fix retention before scaling acquisition.

2. Hacks without strategy: Randomly trying tactics without a growth model. Start with a framework (AARRR, growth loops) and test within it.

3. Ignoring unit economics: Growth hacking that ignores CAC and LTV. Cheap growth that attracts low-value users is worse than no growth.

4. Spammy tactics: Mass cold emails, fake reviews, aggressive pop-ups. These damage brand and get you banned.

5. One-person growth team: Growth hacking requires product, engineering, marketing, and data. It can't be one person's side project.

6. Copying without adapting: "Dropbox did referrals so we should too." Their audience, product, and timing were different. Adapt tactics to your context.
