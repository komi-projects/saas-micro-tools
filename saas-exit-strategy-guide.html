🚪 Exit Strategy

Plan your exit. Maximize value. Ensure a smooth transition.

Why Plan Your Exit Early

You don't build a SaaS to run it forever. Whether you sell in 2 years or 10, planning your exit from day one maximizes valuation and ensures you're ready when opportunity knocks. Companies that prepare sell for 20-40% more than those that scramble.

Exit planning isn't about wanting to leave — it's about building a valuable asset. A SaaS that's attractive to buyers is also attractive to customers, employees, and investors. The same metrics that drive valuation also drive sustainable growth.

Exit Options

🏢 Strategic Acquisition
Acquired by a larger company in your industry. They want your technology, customers, or team. Typically 5-10x revenue for SaaS. Examples: Salesforce acquiring Slack, Microsoft acquiring GitHub.
💰 Private Equity (PE)
PE firms buy mature SaaS companies (usually $5M+ ARR) and optimize for cash flow. They keep the team, grow the business, and sell again in 3-5 years. Valuation: 3-7x EBITDA or 3-5x revenue. Less strategic premium, more financial engineering.
📈 IPO (Initial Public Offering)
Rare and prestigious. Requires $100M+ ARR, profitability or clear path to it, strong governance, and market conditions. Examples: Snowflake, Datadog, Zoom. The hardest exit but potentially the most lucrative.
🤝 Merger of Equals
Combine with a similar-sized company. Shared control, pooled resources, larger market presence. Less common in SaaS but happens in crowded markets (e.g., Martech consolidation).
🏠 Management Buyout (MBO)
Your team buys the company from you. Financing from banks or PE. You get liquidity, team gets ownership, company continues. Good for founders who want to step back but keep the legacy alive.
💵 Dividend Recap
Take a loan against the company's cash flow and pay yourself a dividend. You retain ownership but extract cash. Common in PE-backed companies. Risk: adds debt to the balance sheet.

What Drives SaaS Valuation

• Growth rate: #1 factor. 100%+ YoY growth commands 10x+ revenue multiples. 20% growth gets 3-5x.

• Revenue scale: $1M ARR vs. $10M ARR vs. $50M ARR. Larger companies get higher multiples.

• Profitability: Path to profitability matters. Burning $2M/month on $5M ARR is scary. Burning $500K on $10M ARR with 80% gross margin is fine.

• Retention: NRR > 120% is gold. Logo churn < 5% annually. Net negative churn commands premium multiples.

• Market size: TAM must support a $1B+ outcome for strategic buyers and PE.

• Defensibility: Moat. Network effects, switching costs, proprietary data, brand. The harder to replicate, the higher the premium.

• Team quality: Buyers acquire teams as much as products. Strong engineering and leadership = higher valuation.

• Clean financials: Audited financials, clear cap table, no legal disputes. Messy books reduce valuation or kill deals.

Preparing for Exit

2-3 years before target exit:

• Fix the fundamentals: Get to sustainable unit economics (LTV:CAC > 3, CAC payback < 12 months). Improve gross margins to > 75%. Reduce churn to < 5% annually.

• Build the team: Can the company run without you? Hire a strong COO, VP Sales, VP Product. Buyers fear founder-dependent businesses.

• Clean up the books: Switch to accrual accounting. Hire a CFO or fractional CFO. Get audited financials (required for $10M+ exits).

• Document everything: IP assignments, employment contracts, customer contracts, vendor agreements. Missing documentation kills deals or triggers indemnity.

• Legal hygiene: Resolve disputes. Clean cap table (no missing signatures, no ambiguous equity). Ensure all code is properly licensed (no GPL violations).

1 year before exit:

• Investment bank: Hire an advisor for $5M+ exits. They run the process, find buyers, negotiate terms. Cost: 2-5% of transaction value.

• Data room: Prepare a virtual data room with financials, contracts, legal docs, product metrics, team info. Organized data rooms signal professionalism.

• Story: Craft the narrative. Why now? Why this buyer? What's the vision? Buyers pay premiums for compelling stories.

• Confidentiality: NDAs for all discussions. Leaks damage morale and customer relationships.

The Exit Process

1. Teaser: Anonymous one-page summary sent to potential buyers. ARR, growth rate, market, and rough asking price. No confidential information.

2. NDA + Information Memorandum: Detailed 20-30 page document with financials, product, team, and market. Shared after NDA signed.

3. Initial Bids: Buyers submit non-binding indications of interest (IOI) with valuation range and structure (cash vs. stock, earnouts).

4. Management Presentations: Top 3-5 buyers meet the team, see the product, ask questions. This is sales — sell the vision and the team.

5. Final Bids: Binding offers with detailed terms. Negotiate price, structure, reps and warranties, indemnity, and transition period.

6. Exclusivity + Due Diligence: Winner gets 30-60 day exclusivity. They verify everything you claimed. This is where deals die — be honest upfront.

7. Closing: Sign definitive agreement, transfer funds, announce publicly (if required). Integration begins.

Timeline: 6-12 months from start to close. Complex deals (IPOs, cross-border) can take 18-24 months.

Deal Structure

Cash vs. Stock:

• All cash: Clean exit. You walk away with money. No future risk. Lower total value.

• All stock: Higher nominal value but dependent on buyer's stock performance. Risky if buyer's stock drops.

• Mixed: 50-80% cash, remainder stock. Balances certainty with upside.

Earnouts:

Portion of price contingent on post-acquisition performance. "$10M upfront + $5M if ARR grows 30% in year 1." Risky for seller — you don't control the company post-close. Try to minimize or eliminate earnouts.

Escrow:

10-15% of purchase price held for 12-18 months to cover indemnity claims. You'll get most of it back if you were honest in diligence.

Retained equity / Second bite:

Keep 10-20% equity and stay as CEO or advisor. You participate in the next exit. Common in PE deals. Higher total return over 3-5 years but less immediate liquidity.

Post-Exit Considerations

• Taxes: Capital gains vs. ordinary income. Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) can eliminate federal tax on first $10M. Plan 5 years ahead for QSBS eligibility.

• Team: Retention bonuses for key employees (paid by buyer). Be transparent about who's staying and who's leaving.

• Customers: Communicate early and honestly. "We're joining forces with X to deliver more value." Address concerns proactively.

• Non-compete: You'll likely sign a 2-3 year non-compete. Negotiate scope (geography, market segment, technology).

• Transition: Plan to stay 6-12 months post-close. Knowledge transfer, relationship handoff, integration support. Longer for founder-led companies.

• Next chapter: What's your post-exit plan? Investing? Angel investing? Next startup? Sabbatical? Have a plan before you close — post-exit depression is real.
