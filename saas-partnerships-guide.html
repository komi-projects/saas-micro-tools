🤝 Partnerships

Build alliances that multiply growth.

Why Partnerships Matter

Partnerships are the fastest way to scale without spending more on ads. A single strategic partnership can open access to thousands of qualified customers, add credibility through association, and create revenue streams that compound over time. In SaaS, partnerships are not optional — they're a growth multiplier.

The best partnerships create value for three parties: you, your partner, and the customer. If any side loses, the partnership fails. Great partnerships feel obvious in hindsight — "Of course these two companies work together."

This guide covers the types of SaaS partnerships, how to identify and evaluate partners, how to structure deals, and how to manage partnerships for long-term success.

Types of SaaS Partnerships

1. Technology / Integration Partners
Products that connect and create joint value. • Example: Slack + Zoom — video calls inside chat • Example: Notion + Figma — embed designs in docs • Value: Enhanced product utility, shared customers, co-marketing • Revenue model: Free (drives retention) or revenue share (if joint offering) • Best for: Products with APIs, platforms, and marketplaces
2. Channel / Reseller Partners
Partners who sell your product to their customers. • Example: AWS Marketplace — ISVs sell through AWS • Example: Agencies reselling SaaS to their clients • Value: Expanded sales reach, local market access, bundled offerings • Revenue model: 15-30% commission on sales • Best for: Enterprise SaaS, complex products, geographic expansion
3. Strategic / Co-Marketing Partners
Joint marketing without deep product integration. • Example: Co-hosted webinars, joint blog posts, bundled content • Example: "The Ultimate SaaS Stack" featuring 5 complementary tools • Value: Shared audience, cost splitting, credibility boost • Revenue model: Usually free, sometimes lead exchange • Best for: Same target market, non-competing products
4. Affiliate Partners
Individuals or companies who refer customers for commission. • Example: Bloggers, YouTubers, industry influencers • Example: Complementary SaaS companies ("Use our tool + theirs") • Value: Low-risk acquisition, performance-based cost • Revenue model: 20-40% recurring commission (or one-time bounty) • Best for: Self-serve products, content-heavy audiences, broad markets
5. API / Platform Partners
Developers building on your platform. • Example: Shopify App Store — 8,000+ apps built by developers • Example: Salesforce AppExchange — enterprise ecosystem • Value: Platform stickiness, ecosystem growth, revenue from app fees • Revenue model: Revenue share (70/30 or 80/20), listing fees, API fees • Best for: Platform products, infrastructure SaaS, developer tools
6. Data / Integration Partners
Sharing or enriching data between platforms. • Example: Clearbit enriching CRM records with company data • Example: Segment collecting data and sending to 300+ tools • Value: Data completeness, workflow automation, reduced manual entry • Revenue model: Revenue share, data licensing, joint packages • Best for: Data-heavy products, CRM, analytics, enrichment tools

Identifying the Right Partners

1. Customer overlap:

The best partners serve the same customers you do, with a non-competing product. Ask: "What other tools do our best customers use daily?" If 60% of your customers also use Asana, Asana might be a good integration partner.

2. Complementary value:

The partner's product should make yours more valuable, not replace it. A CRM + email marketing tool = complementary. A CRM + another CRM = competitive.

3. Similar stage:

Partnerships work best when both companies are at similar growth stages. A 3-person startup partnering with Salesforce is unbalanced. A 3-person startup partnering with another 3-person startup is a fair fight.

4. Shared values:

Partnerships are relationships. Do you trust this company? Do they treat customers well? Will they prioritize the partnership? A bad partner damages your brand by association.

5. Technical fit:

For integration partnerships, assess API quality, documentation, support responsiveness, and engineering culture. A partner with a terrible API will cost you months of engineering time.

6. Strategic intent:

Is this partnership a core strategy or a side project? If the partner's CEO doesn't care about the partnership, it will die when their PM leaves. Look for partnerships in the partner's public roadmap and marketing.

Partnership Structure and Terms

Revenue share:

• Standard: 15-30% for channel partners, 20-40% for affiliates • Recurring vs. one-time: Recurring aligns long-term incentives • Tracking: UTM parameters, referral codes, cookies, API attribution • Payment: Monthly or quarterly. Automate where possible. • Minimums: Some partnerships require minimum sales volumes

Co-marketing:

• Joint content: Blog posts, ebooks, webinars (split costs and leads) • Cross-promotion: Email to each other's lists, social media shoutouts • Events: Co-sponsor conferences, host joint meetups • PR: Joint press releases, media outreach • Commitments: Document who does what by when. Verbal agreements fail.

Product integration:

• Scope: What features are included? What's out of scope? • Timeline: Milestones with dates. Pad by 50% for reality. • Resources: Who builds what? Who maintains it? • Support: How do joint customers get help? Escalation paths? • Marketing: How will the integration be announced? To whom? • SLA: Uptime requirements, response times, data handling

Exclusivity:

• Avoid exclusivity unless there's massive value. Limiting your options is dangerous. • If exclusive: Define scope narrowly ("exclusive in the healthcare vertical" not "exclusive forever") • Set time limits (6-12 months) with renewal options • Include performance clauses (must generate X revenue to maintain exclusivity)

Partnership Lifecycle

1. Discovery: Identify potential partners. Research their product, audience, and partnership history. Warm intros beat cold outreach. Use mutual connections.

2. Qualification: Assess fit. Do they serve your target customers? Are they partnership-minded? What's their reputation? Run a small test before committing big.

3. Negotiation: Define terms. Revenue split, responsibilities, timeline, marketing commitments, success metrics. Get it in writing. Even a simple one-page agreement prevents disputes.

4. Launch: Execute the integration or go-to-market plan. Coordinate announcements. Train support teams. Prepare FAQ for joint customers.

5. Growth: Optimize the partnership. A/B test co-marketing. Improve the integration. Expand to new use cases. Regular check-ins (monthly or quarterly).

6. Review / Sunset: Assess performance. Is the partnership meeting goals? If not, diagnose why. If it can't be fixed, sunset gracefully. Don't ghost partners.

Partnership Mistakes

1. Partnerships without strategy: "Let's partner with everyone!" 50 weak partnerships are worse than 5 strong ones. Be selective.

2. Misaligned incentives: Your partner makes money on implementation, you make money on subscriptions. They want complex custom setups, you want fast self-serve. Align incentives or the partnership will break.

3. No ownership: Partnerships need an internal owner. Someone whose job includes making this partnership successful. Without an owner, partnerships die from neglect.

4. Over-promising: "We'll build the integration in 2 weeks!" Then it takes 3 months. Under-promise, over-deliver. Partnership trust is hard to rebuild.

5. Ignoring the long tail: Partnerships need ongoing care. Regular check-ins, joint marketing, feature updates. A launched partnership that never gets maintained becomes a liability.

6. Competitive creep: Your partner launches a competing feature. Now what? Build contingency plans. Don't bet the farm on a single partnership.

Partnership Checklist

☐ Partner identified and qualified (customer overlap, values, stage) ☐ Partnership type selected (integration, channel, co-marketing, affiliate) ☐ Revenue model defined and documented ☐ Technical assessment completed (for integration partners) ☐ Agreement signed (even simple one-pager) ☐ Internal owner assigned ☐ Launch plan with dates and responsibilities ☐ Support team trained on joint offering ☐ Marketing coordinated (announcement, content, joint campaigns) ☐ Tracking and attribution configured ☐ Success metrics defined and baselined ☐ Regular check-in schedule established ☐ Exit plan defined (how to sunset if needed) ☐ Performance reviewed quarterly
