Why Compliance Matters for SaaS

Compliance isn't just paperwork — it's the difference between closing enterprise deals and losing them. When a Fortune 500 company evaluates SaaS vendors, they don't just look at features and pricing. They look at security certifications, data handling practices, and regulatory compliance. "Are you SOC 2 Type II certified?" is often the first question after "What does your product do?"

Compliance is also about risk management. GDPR fines can reach 4% of global revenue. PCI-DSS violations can mean losing the ability to process credit cards. HIPAA breaches can result in criminal charges. Investing in compliance early protects you from existential threats later.

The good news: most compliance frameworks overlap significantly. If you build strong security and privacy practices, achieving SOC 2, GDPR, and other certifications becomes much easier. This guide covers the major compliance frameworks SaaS companies need and how to approach them efficiently.

SOC 2 (Trust Services Criteria)

What it is: Audit report on your security controls, issued by a CPA firm. The gold standard for B2B SaaS.

Two types:

• Type I: Point-in-time assessment. Faster, cheaper, less valuable. Good for startups needing quick credibility. • Type II: Controls tested over 3-12 months. More expensive, more valuable. Required by most enterprises.

Five Trust Services Criteria:

• Security (required): Protection against unauthorized access • Availability: System uptime and reliability • Processing integrity: Data processing is complete, valid, accurate, timely • Confidentiality: Data is protected as committed • Privacy: Personal information is collected, used, retained, disclosed, and disposed of properly

How to get it:

• Gap assessment: Identify what's missing vs. SOC 2 requirements • Remediation: Fix gaps (access controls, logging, backup testing, etc.) • Audit: CPA firm reviews evidence over observation period (Type II) • Report: Receive SOC 2 report to share with customers • Timeline: 3-6 months for Type I, 6-12 months for Type II • Cost: $10K-50K for audit, plus internal effort

Tools to help: Vanta, Drata, Secureframe, Sprinto (automate evidence collection)

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

What it is: EU regulation on data protection and privacy. Applies if you process EU residents' data, regardless of your location.

Key requirements:

• Lawful basis: You need a legal reason to process data (consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, legitimate interests) • Consent: Freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous, easily withdrawn • Data minimization: Collect only what's necessary • Purpose limitation: Use data only for stated purposes • Storage limitation: Delete when no longer needed • Accuracy: Keep data up to date, correct errors • Integrity and confidentiality: Secure processing • Accountability: Document compliance, appoint DPO if required • Rights: Users can access, rectify, erase, restrict processing, object, port data • Breach notification: Notify supervisory authority within 72 hours • Cross-border transfers: Safeguards for data leaving EU (SCCs, adequacy decisions)

Implementation:

• Privacy policy: Clear, comprehensive, accessible • Cookie consent: Banner with opt-in for non-essential cookies • Data processing agreements (DPA): With all subprocessors • Record of processing activities: Document what you process and why • Data protection impact assessment (DPIA): For high-risk processing • Data subject rights workflow: How to handle access/deletion requests • Breach response plan: 72-hour notification process

HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act)

What it is: US regulation protecting health information. Applies if you process PHI (Protected Health Information).

Key requirements:

• Business Associate Agreement (BAA): Required with all healthcare customers • Administrative safeguards: Risk analysis, workforce training, access management • Physical safeguards: Facility access controls, workstation security • Technical safeguards: Access controls, audit controls, integrity controls, transmission security • Breach notification: Notify affected individuals, HHS, and media (if >500 people)

Implementation:

• BAA template: Standard agreement for healthcare customers • Encryption: At rest and in transit for all PHI • Access controls: Role-based, minimum necessary principle • Audit logging: All PHI access logged and reviewed • Data backup and recovery: Tested regularly • Workforce training: Annual HIPAA training for all employees

PCI-DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard)

What it is: Security standard for handling credit card data. Required if you store, process, or transmit cardholder data.

Key requirements (12 requirements):

1. Firewall configuration: Protect cardholder data environment 2. No default passwords: Change all system defaults 3. Stored data protection: Encrypt stored cardholder data 4. Encrypted transmission: Encrypt across open/public networks 5. Anti-virus: Maintain and update regularly 6. Secure systems: Patch management, secure development 7. Need-to-know access: Restrict by business need 8. Unique IDs: Assign unique IDs to each person with computer access 9. Physical access: Restrict to cardholder data environment 10. Access logging: Log and monitor all access to network resources and cardholder data 11. Security testing: Regular vulnerability scans and penetration tests 12. Information security policy: Maintain a policy that addresses information security

Best practice: Don't store cardholder data. Use tokenization (Stripe, Braintree) so you never touch raw card numbers.

Other Important Frameworks

CCPA/CPRA (California)
California Consumer Privacy Act. Similar to GDPR but focused on sale of data. • Disclose data collection and sale • Allow opt-out of data sale • Delete data on request • Non-discrimination for privacy choices
ISO 27001
International standard for information security management. • More comprehensive than SOC 2 • Required by some international enterprises • 3-year certification cycle with annual surveillance audits
FedRAMP (US Federal)
Required for SaaS selling to US federal agencies. • Three levels: Low, Moderate, High (based on data sensitivity) • Extensive documentation and continuous monitoring • Typically 12-18 months, $250K+ cost
COPPA (Children's Privacy)
If your product is for children under 13 or you know you collect their data. • Verifiable parental consent required • Specific data minimization rules • Parental rights to review and delete data

Compliance Strategy

1. Start with SOC 2 Type I: Fastest path to enterprise credibility. Builds foundation for everything else.

2. Layer GDPR on top: Most overlap with SOC 2 (security, access controls, logging). Add privacy-specific controls (consent, data subject rights, cross-border transfers).

3. Add industry-specific as needed: HIPAA for healthcare, PCI-DSS if you store cards, FedRAMP for federal.

4. Automate evidence collection: Use compliance platforms (Vanta, Drata) to continuously collect evidence. Manual evidence gathering is a full-time job.

5. Build compliance into engineering: Infrastructure as code, automated testing, security scanning, audit logging — make compliance a byproduct of good engineering.

6. Customer trust center: Publish security whitepaper, SOC 2 report access, penetration test summaries, and compliance certifications in one place.
