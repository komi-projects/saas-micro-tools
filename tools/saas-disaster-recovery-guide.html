🌪️ Disaster Recovery

Plan for the worst. Recover fast. Stay alive.

What is Disaster Recovery?

Disaster recovery (DR) is the process of restoring IT infrastructure and operations after a catastrophic event — natural disaster, cyberattack, data center failure, or human error. For SaaS companies, DR isn't optional. Your customers depend on your service being available.

The difference between incident management and disaster recovery: incident management handles partial failures (one server down, one service degraded). DR handles total failure (entire data center offline, database corrupted, ransomware encrypted everything).

Disaster Scenarios

🔥 Infrastructure Failure
Data center outage, cloud region failure, network partition. AWS us-east-1 has had multiple multi-hour outages. If all your infrastructure is in one region, you're down when it's down.
🗑️ Data Loss/Corruption
Database corruption from bad migration, accidental DROP TABLE, ransomware encryption, storage failure. Without good backups and tested recovery, this is company-ending.
🔓 Security Breach
Ransomware, data exfiltration, compromised credentials. You may need to rebuild systems from scratch, rotate all secrets, and notify customers. Incident response + DR together.
👤 Human Error
Developer deletes production database. Ops engineer misconfigures firewall. Deploy script drops wrong table. Most outages are human-caused, not system-caused.
🏢 Physical Disaster
Earthquake, flood, fire at primary data center. Less common with cloud, but still relevant if you have on-premise infrastructure or physical offices.

DR Planning Framework

1. Business Impact Analysis (BIA)

Identify critical systems and their downtime impact:

• Tier 1 (Revenue-critical): Payment processing, core API, database. Downtime = immediate revenue loss. • Tier 2 (Customer-facing): Dashboard, reporting, webhooks. Downtime = customer frustration. • Tier 3 (Internal): Admin tools, analytics, billing UI. Downtime = operational impact. • Tier 4 (Non-critical): Marketing site, docs, blog. Downtime = minimal impact.

2. Recovery Objectives

For each tier, define RPO and RTO. Be realistic — DR to meet 5-minute RTO costs 10x more than 4-hour RTO.

3. DR Strategies

• Backup and Restore: Cheapest. Back up to cloud storage, restore when needed. RTO: hours to days. Good for Tier 3-4. • Pilot Light: Core systems running at minimal capacity in secondary region. Scale up during disaster. RTO: 30-60 minutes. Good for Tier 2. • Warm Standby: Full system running in secondary region at reduced capacity. Traffic switched via DNS. RTO: 5-15 minutes. Good for Tier 1. • Hot Standby / Active-Active: Full system running in multiple regions simultaneously. Automatic failover. RTO: seconds to minutes. Expensive. For Tier 1 with high availability requirements.

4. DR Site

Choose a secondary region/geography. If primary is us-east-1, secondary should be us-west-2 (different power grid, different earthquake zone). For global DR, consider cross-cloud (AWS primary, GCP secondary).

DR Runbook

Document step-by-step recovery procedures:

1. Detection: How do you know a disaster occurred? Monitoring alerts, customer reports, manual checks. 2. Declaration: Who declares a disaster? CEO? CTO? On-call engineer? What criteria trigger DR (vs. normal incident response)? 3. Notification: Who gets notified? Customers (status page), team (Slack/Teams), executives (phone), regulators (if required). 4. Recovery: Step-by-step to restore service. Exact commands, exact runbooks, exact infrastructure code. Tested monthly. 5. Verification: How do you confirm service is fully restored? Health checks, synthetic monitoring, spot checks. 6. Post-DR Review: What worked? What didn't? Update DR plan. Similar to post-mortem but for DR.

Testing Your DR Plan

An untested DR plan is a fantasy. Test it:

Tabletop exercise: Walk through the plan on paper. 2 hours, key team members. Identify gaps without touching production.

Functional test: Actually restore from backup to a test environment. Verify data integrity, application functionality, and recovery time. Quarterly.

Full DR drill: Failover to secondary site during low-traffic hours. Run production traffic through DR site for 1-4 hours. Verify everything works. Annually (or biannually for Tier 1).

Chaos engineering: Randomly kill production instances, databases, or services. Verify automatic recovery. Start in staging, move to production with safeguards.

Cloud-Native DR

AWS: RDS Multi-AZ + cross-region read replicas. S3 cross-region replication. Route 53 health checks with failover routing. CloudFormation for infrastructure as code (rebuild in minutes).

GCP: Cloud SQL high availability. Cloud Storage dual-region buckets. Cloud DNS with health checks. Deployment Manager / Terraform for reproducible infrastructure.

Azure: SQL Database geo-replication. Storage geo-redundancy. Traffic Manager for DNS failover. Azure Site Recovery for VM replication.

Multi-cloud: For maximum resilience, primary on AWS, DR on GCP. Complex, expensive, but protects against cloud provider-wide issues (rare but not impossible).

DR Costs vs. Risk

DR is insurance. You pay for protection you hope to never use. Balance cost with risk:

• Startup ($0-1M ARR): Backup and restore. Daily backups to S3, tested monthly. RTO: 4-8 hours. Cost: $50-200/month. • Growth ($1M-10M ARR): Pilot light or warm standby. Cross-region replication, automated failover for database. RTO: 30-60 minutes. Cost: $500-2K/month. • Scale ($10M+ ARR): Hot standby / active-active. Full multi-region deployment, automated failover, load balancing. RTO: seconds to minutes. Cost: $5K-20K/month.

The cost of DR is always less than the cost of a major outage at scale.
