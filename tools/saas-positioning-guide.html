🎯 Positioning

Own a category. Become the obvious choice.

What is Positioning?

Positioning is the deliberate act of defining how you want to be perceived in the minds of your target customers. It's not about changing the product — it's about changing the context in which customers evaluate it. "We're not just another CRM, we're the CRM built for sales teams that hate data entry." That changes who considers you and why.

Positioning answers three questions:

1. For whom? Who is your ideal customer?
2. What's the problem? What pain do they have?
3. Why you? Why are you uniquely suited to solve it?

Without clear positioning, you compete with everyone. With clear positioning, you compete with no one — because you're the only one in your category.

The Positioning Framework

1. Identify your best-fit customers:

Look at your current customers. Who: • Signed up fastest? • Activated easiest? • Upgraded fastest? • Uses the product most? • Refers others? • Has the highest LTV? • Doesn't churn? These are your best-fit customers. Profile them deeply: industry, company size, role, pain points, buying process, tech stack, budget. The more specific, the better. "SaaS companies with 10-50 employees and a sales-led motion" beats "B2B companies."

2. Map the competitive landscape:

Who do your best-fit customers compare you to? Not who you think your competitors are — who do customers actually evaluate alongside you? Ask them: "What else did you consider before choosing us?" Map competitors on two axes: • Price: Low vs. high • Approach: Simple vs. comprehensive Look for the whitespace. Where is nobody playing? That's your opportunity.

3. Define your unique value:

What do you do that competitors can't or won't? • Capabilities: Features or functionality only you have • Approach: A fundamentally different way of solving the problem • Market focus: Serving a segment others ignore • Delivery model: Pricing, packaging, or go-to-market others don't use The best positioning combines multiple dimensions. "We're the only analytics tool designed for mobile game developers that uses AI to predict churn before it happens."

4. Choose your market context:

You can position in existing markets or create new ones: • Head-to-head: "We're better than [leader] at [specific thing]." Risky but can work if you truly are better. • Category creation: "We're not a CRM, we're a revenue operations platform." Creates new category where you're the leader. • Niche focus: "We're the CRM for real estate agents." Owns a segment of an existing market. • Adjacent category: "We're what Slack would be if it was built for customer support teams." Leverages known context. Category creation is hardest but most defensible. Head-to-head is easiest but most competitive.

5. Write your positioning statement:

Template: For [target customer] who [problem statement], [product name] is the [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [alternatives], we [unique differentiation].

Example: Notion
For startup teams who are overwhelmed by scattered docs, wikis, and project tools, Notion is the all-in-one workspace that replaces them all. Unlike Google Docs + Trello + Confluence, we unify everything in one flexible platform.
Example: Superhuman
For executives and founders who live in email, Superhuman is the email client that makes you feel like you have superpowers. Unlike Gmail or Outlook, we're built entirely for speed and keyboard shortcuts.

Positioning Signals

Your positioning should be visible everywhere:

• Homepage headline: The first thing visitors see. Should communicate category and differentiation in 10 words. • Pricing page: Plan names and feature allocation signal who you're for. • Sales deck: First 3 slides establish the problem, the category, and why you're different. • Product features:>/strong> Feature prioritization signals positioning. A "simple" tool shouldn't have 200 settings. • Marketing content: Blog topics, case studies, and social posts reinforce the positioning. • Customer testimonials: Whose quotes you feature signals who you're for. • Partnerships: Who you integrate with signals your ecosystem and category. • Job postings: What roles you hire for signals your priorities.

Consistency is key. If your homepage says "simple" but your product has 50 onboarding steps, the positioning is broken. Every touchpoint must reinforce the same message.

When to Reposition

Positioning isn't forever. Reposition when:

• Market shift: The competitive landscape changed. A new entrant stole your niche, or the category leader moved into your space. • Product evolution: You built features that change who you're for. Started as a tool for marketers, now you're also used by sales. • Customer insight: You discovered your best-fit customers are different from who you initially targeted. • Scale: You outgrew your niche and need a bigger category to support growth. • Acquisition: You acquired or merged with another product and need unified positioning. • Brand baggage: Negative associations with your current positioning that you can't shake.

Repositioning is risky. It confuses existing customers and can feel like you're abandoning your core. Do it deliberately, communicate clearly, and give customers time to adjust.

Positioning Mistakes

1. Being everything to everyone: "We help businesses of all sizes in all industries." This means you help no one specifically. Specificity wins.

2. Feature-based positioning: "We have the most integrations." Features can be copied. Positioning on outcomes: "We save you 10 hours a week on reporting."

3. Copying the leader: "We're like [leader] but cheaper." This positions you as a discount version. Nobody aspires to be the cheap alternative.

4. Over-promising: "The only platform you'll ever need." Users are skeptical of grandiose claims. Under-promise, over-deliver.

5. Internal focus: "Built with React and GraphQL." Customers don't care about your tech stack. They care about their problem being solved.

6. Jargon: "AI-powered synergistic cloud-native platform." Buzzwords signal insecurity. Plain language signals confidence.

Positioning Checklist

☐ Best-fit customer profile documented and specific ☐ Competitive landscape mapped (actual competitors, not aspirational) ☐ Unique differentiation identified and defensible ☐ Positioning statement written (fits the template) ☐ Category chosen (head-to-head, niche, or new category) ☐ Homepage headline communicates positioning in 10 words ☐ Pricing reinforces who you're for ☐ Sales deck opens with problem + category + differentiation ☐ Marketing content reinforces positioning consistently ☐ Product features aligned with positioning (simple vs. comprehensive) ☐ Team understands and can articulate positioning ☐ Positioning tested with 5+ target customers (do they get it?) ☐ Repositioning trigger defined (when will we reconsider?)
