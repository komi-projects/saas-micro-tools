🎧 Support

Turn tickets into loyalty. Every interaction is a retention opportunity.

Support as a Growth Engine

Support isn't a cost center — it's a growth engine. Every support interaction is a chance to save a customer, gather product feedback, and create an advocate. Companies that invest in support see higher NPS, lower churn, and more expansion revenue.

The math: If it costs $500 to acquire a customer and $50 to retain them through great support, support is 10x more efficient than sales. Yet many companies underinvest in it, treating it as a necessary evil instead of a competitive advantage.

Support Channels

💬 Live Chat
Best for: Quick questions, real-time troubleshooting, sales inquiries. Response time: < 2 minutes during business hours. Tools: Intercom, Drift, Zendesk Chat, Crisp. Cost: Medium (requires real-time agents). Satisfaction: Highest. Instant resolution feels premium.
📧 Email / Tickets
Best for: Complex issues, detailed documentation, non-urgent requests. Response time: < 4 hours for urgent, < 24 hours for standard. Tools: Zendesk, Freshdesk, Help Scout, Front. Cost: Low (can be async, offshore-friendly). Satisfaction: Good if expectations are clear.
📞 Phone
Best for: Enterprise accounts, complex escalations, relationship building. Response time: < 5 minutes during business hours. Tools: Aircall, Dialpad, Zendesk Talk. Cost: High (dedicated agents, training). Satisfaction: Highest for enterprise. Expected by $50K+ ACV customers.
📚 Self-Service (KB + Community)
Best for: How-to questions, common issues, feature discovery. Response time: Instant. Tools: Notion, GitBook, Zendesk Guide, Discourse. Cost: Low (one-time creation, ongoing maintenance). Satisfaction: High for simple questions. 70% of users prefer self-service for basic issues.
🤖 AI / Chatbot
Best for: Deflection (answering common questions before they reach humans). Response time: Instant. Tools: Intercom Fin, Zendesk AI, Forethought, Ada. Cost: Medium (setup + ongoing training). Satisfaction: Improving rapidly. Best when it seamlessly escalates to humans.

Support Metrics

• First response time: Time from ticket creation to first human response. < 1 hour for chat, < 4 hours for email. Not the same as resolution time. • Resolution time: Time from creation to close. < 24 hours for 80% of tickets. Track by priority. • CSAT (Customer Satisfaction): % of customers who rate support positively. > 90% is excellent. Survey after every interaction. • NPS (Net Promoter Score): Would they recommend you? > 50 for B2B SaaS. Survey quarterly. • Ticket volume: Tickets per customer per month. Trending down = product improving or docs getting better. • First contact resolution (FCR): % resolved in one interaction. > 70% is good. • Backlog: Open tickets > 48 hours old. Should be near zero. • Agent utilization: % of time agents spend on tickets. 70-80% is healthy. > 90% = burnout risk. • Cost per ticket: Total support cost / tickets handled. Track efficiency. • Escalation rate: % tickets escalated to engineering/management. < 10% is healthy.

Tiered Support Model

Tier 1 (L1): First line of defense. Handles 70-80% of tickets. • Password resets, billing questions, how-to, basic troubleshooting • Uses macros and knowledge base • Escalates to L2 if can't resolve in 15 minutes • Usually outsourced or entry-level hires

Tier 2 (L2): Technical support. Handles 15-25% of tickets. • Complex troubleshooting, bug verification, API issues, integration problems • Has access to logs and internal tools • Can write SQL queries, read error traces • Escalates to engineering for confirmed bugs

Tier 3 (L3 / Engineering): Product and engineering. Handles 5-10% of tickets. • Bug fixes, feature requests, architectural issues • Not a support function — it's development prioritized by customer impact • L3 should have SLA: critical bugs fixed within 24-48 hours

Reducing Support Volume

The best support ticket is the one that never gets created:

• Better onboarding: 30% of tickets are "how do I..." questions. Better onboarding = fewer tickets. • In-app guidance: Contextual help at the point of need. Tooltips, walkthroughs, inline docs. • Knowledge base: Searchable, comprehensive, up-to-date. Video tutorials for complex workflows. • Proactive communication: Email users about known issues before they ask. "We're aware of X and working on it." • Self-service password reset: Sounds basic, but 10-15% of tickets are password-related. • Status page: Reduces "is it down?" tickets during incidents. • Product improvements: Analyze ticket categories. Top 3 categories = top 3 product priorities. • Autocomplete in search: Help users find answers before they submit tickets.

Support Team Structure by Stage

Startup (< $1M ARR):

• Founder handles support directly (or shares with early team) • 1 shared inbox (support@company.com) • Basic FAQ page • Response time: Best effort (within 24 hours) • No dedicated team yet

Growth ($1M-$10M ARR):

• Hire first support person (generalist, handles everything) • Support tool (Zendesk, Intercom) • Knowledge base with 50+ articles • Response SLA: < 4 hours email, < 2 minutes chat • Support metrics tracked monthly

Scale ($10M+ ARR):

• Support team with manager • Tiered support (L1, L2) • Multiple channels (chat, email, phone for enterprise) • 24/7 coverage (via offshore team or shifts) • Dedicated enterprise support reps (1:many ratio) • Support QA (review random tickets for quality) • Knowledge base with 200+ articles, video library, community forum

The Support Philosophy

Empathy first: The customer is frustrated. They don't care about your internal processes. Acknowledge their frustration, then solve the problem. "I understand how frustrating that is. Let me fix it right now."

Speed second: Fast response matters more than perfect response. A quick "I'm looking into this and will update you in 2 hours" beats silence for 4 hours followed by a perfect answer.

Resolution third: Actually fix the problem. Follow up to confirm. Close the loop. Don't leave tickets hanging.

Feedback always: Every ticket is product feedback. Categorize tickets. Share weekly summaries with product team. The top 3 ticket categories should be the top 3 product priorities.
