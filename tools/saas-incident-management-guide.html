🚨 Incident Management

Respond fast. Communicate clearly. Learn always.

What is Incident Management?

Incident management is the process of responding to and resolving unplanned disruptions to your service. In SaaS, incidents are inevitable — databases fail, deploys break, third-party APIs go down. What separates good teams from great ones is how they respond.

The goal isn't zero incidents (impossible). The goal is: detect fast, respond effectively, communicate transparently, and prevent recurrence. Every incident is a learning opportunity.

Incident Lifecycle

1. Detection
Automated alerts (monitoring), customer reports (support tickets), or manual checks. The faster you detect, the less impact. Target: detect via monitoring before customers notice. This requires comprehensive alerting on business metrics, not just infrastructure.
2. Triage
Determine severity and impact. How many users affected? Which features broken? Revenue impact? Assign severity: SEV1 (all users, complete outage), SEV2 (significant subset, major feature broken), SEV3 (minor impact, workaround available), SEV4 (no customer impact, internal issue).
3. Response
Assemble response team (incident commander, technical lead, communications lead). Execute runbook. Communicate status every 15-30 minutes. Don't fix in silence — keep stakeholders updated even if you don't have a solution yet.
4. Resolution
Restore service, not necessarily fix root cause. If a bad deploy caused the issue, roll back first. Fix the bug later. "Service restored" and "incident closed" are different milestones. Verify fix: test in production, monitor for 1 hour before declaring all-clear.
5. Post-Mortem
Within 48 hours of incident close. What happened? Timeline of events. Root cause analysis (5 Whys). Impact assessment. Action items with owners and deadlines. Share internally (all-hands or Slack). Consider publishing externally for SEV1 incidents (builds trust).

Incident Communication

Internal: Dedicated incident Slack/Teams channel. War room (video call) for SEV1/SEV2. Clear roles: Incident Commander (coordinates), Scribe (takes notes), Technical Lead (fixes), Communications Lead (external updates).

External (customers): Status page update within 5 minutes of SEV1 declaration. Update every 15-30 minutes until resolved. Be specific but not overly technical. "Payment processing is experiencing delays" not "Database connection pool exhausted." Apologize, explain, commit to improvement.

Post-resolution: Final update on status page. Follow-up email to affected customers. Post-mortem published (internally or externally). Action items tracked to completion.

Incident Metrics

• MTTD (Mean Time to Detection): How fast you find out. Target: < 5 minutes via automated alerts.

• MTTR (Mean Time to Response): How fast someone starts working on it. Target: < 15 minutes for SEV1, < 1 hour for SEV2.

• MTTF (Mean Time to Fix/Recovery): How fast service is restored. Target varies by severity.

• Incident frequency: Incidents per month. Trending down = improving reliability.

• Post-mortem completion rate: % of incidents with published post-mortem within 48 hours. Target: 100%.

• Action item completion: % of post-mortem action items completed on time. Target: > 80%.

Incident Tools

Paging: PagerDuty, Opsgenie, VictorOps — on-call scheduling, escalation policies, alert routing.

Status Page: Statuspage.io, Instatus, Cachet — public communication during incidents.

War Room: Slack huddles, Zoom, Google Meet — synchronous collaboration during incidents.

Runbooks: Notion, Confluence, GitHub Wiki — step-by-step incident response procedures.

Post-mortems: Blameless post-mortem templates, incident tracking (Jira, Linear).

Building a Blameless Culture

Blameless post-mortems are essential. The goal is to understand what happened and how to prevent it, not to find who to blame. People make mistakes. Systems should prevent those mistakes from causing incidents.

Blameless language: "The deploy script didn't verify the database migration" not "John didn't check the migration." "The alerting threshold was too high" not "The on-call engineer missed the alert."

Psychological safety: Engineers must feel safe to report incidents, admit mistakes, and suggest improvements. If people fear punishment, they'll hide problems until they become disasters.
