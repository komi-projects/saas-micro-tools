🔬 User Research

Build what customers actually want.

Why User Research is Non-Negotiable

The #1 reason SaaS products fail isn't technical — it's that nobody wants them. Founders build what they think customers need, launch it, and hear crickets. User research prevents this by revealing what customers actually want, how they currently solve the problem, and what would make them switch to your solution.

The best SaaS companies treat user research as a continuous activity, not a one-time event. They interview customers monthly, survey quarterly, and analyze behavior daily. They build feedback loops into every product decision. And they know that the cost of talking to users is trivial compared to the cost of building the wrong thing.

This guide covers the methods, tools, and processes for research that drives product decisions and revenue.

User Research Methods

1. Customer Interviews
The gold standard for understanding customer needs. • Frequency: 5-10 interviews per month (continuous, not one-time) • Duration: 30-45 minutes, scheduled in advance • Format: Open-ended questions, let them talk 80% of the time • Recording: With permission, for note-taking and sharing • Incentives: $50-100 gift card or free month of product • Recruitment: Existing customers, trial users who didn't convert, churned users
2. Surveys
Scale your understanding to hundreds or thousands of users. • NPS: "How likely are you to recommend us?" (quarterly) • CSAT: "How satisfied were you with [interaction]?" (after key touchpoints) • CES: "How easy was it to [task]?" (measure friction) • Product-Market Fit: "How disappointed would you be if [product] went away?" • Feature requests: "What should we build next?" (prioritize with voting) • Tools: Typeform, SurveyMonkey, Delighted, Pendo, UserVoice
3. Usability Testing
Watch users struggle with your product (it's humbling and essential). • Task-based: "Show me how you would [achieve goal]" • Think-aloud: Ask them to narrate their thoughts while using the product • Unmoderated: Tools like UserTesting, Maze, or Lookback (async) • Moderated: You guide them through tasks, ask follow-up questions • Frequency: Before major releases, after onboarding changes, quarterly • Metrics: Task success rate, time on task, error rate, SUS score
4. Behavioral Analytics
What users do is often different from what they say. • Funnel analysis: Where do users drop off? (signup → activation → paid) • Cohort analysis: How do different user groups behave over time? • Session recordings: Watch real users navigate (Hotjar, FullStory, LogRocket) • Heatmaps: Where do users click, scroll, and ignore? (Hotjar, Crazy Egg) • Feature adoption: Which features are used? Which are ignored? • Tools: Mixpanel, Amplitude, Heap, Pendo, Hotjar
5. Support and Sales Feedback
Your frontline teams hear what customers actually want. • Support tickets: Categorize top issues (reveals product gaps) • Sales calls: "Why did they say no?" (objection analysis) • Churn reasons: Exit surveys and interviews (learn why they leave) • Win/loss analysis: Why did they choose you? Or a competitor? • Feedback loop: Weekly report from support/sales to product team • Voice of customer: Curate quotes and stories for product decisions

Interview Techniques

1. Before the interview:

• Research the participant: Their company, role, usage history • Prepare questions: 5-7 open-ended questions, not a script • Test tech: Zoom, recording, screen sharing — test 10 minutes early • Set expectations: "This is not a sales call — we're here to learn from you" • Incentive: Confirm the gift card/compensation they'll receive

2. During the interview:

• Start broad: "Tell me about your role and how you currently [do task]" • Dig deeper: "Why is that important to you?" "What happens if you don't?" • Avoid leading: "Don't you think [feature] would be helpful?" → "How do you feel about [feature area]?" • Embrace silence: After they answer, wait 3 seconds — they'll often add more • Take notes: But stay engaged — eye contact, nodding, brief affirmations • End with open: "What else should we have asked?" "What's one thing we should fix?"

3. After the interview:

• Send thank you: Within 24 hours, include promised incentive • Debrief: Write top 3 insights within 30 minutes while memory is fresh • Share: Send recording/notes to product team (not just research team) • Tag: Categorize insights (feature request, pain point, workflow, persona) • Follow up: If they mentioned something specific, update them when you build it

Survey Design Best Practices

1. Keep it short: 5-10 questions max. Every extra question reduces completion rate by 10%.

2. Start easy: First question should be simple (rating, multiple choice). Save open-ended for later when they're committed.

3. One question at a time: Don't ask "How satisfied are you with speed AND reliability?" — one metric per question.

4. Use scales consistently: 1-5 or 1-10, but stick to one scale across surveys. Label the endpoints ("Very dissatisfied" to "Very satisfied").

5. Avoid bias: "How much do you love our product?" → "How would you rate your experience with our product?"

6. Segment by behavior: Send different surveys to power users vs. casual users vs. churned users. Their needs are different.

7. Close the loop: After collecting feedback, tell users what you did with it. "You asked for X — we built it. Here's how to try it."

Research-Driven Product Decisions

1. Feature prioritization:

• Impact: How many users asked for this? How important is it to them? • Effort: How hard to build? What's the opportunity cost? • Alignment: Does this fit our strategy and differentiation? • Data + intuition: Research informs, but vision decides • Framework: RICE (Reach × Impact × Confidence / Effort)

2. Validate before building:

• Fake door test: Put a button for the feature on your site. Count clicks. If nobody clicks, don't build it. • Concierge MVP: Manually do what the feature would do. See if users value it. • Prototype testing: Figma prototype + usability test before writing code. • Landing page test: Create a landing page for the feature. Measure interest. • Wizard of Oz: Behind the scenes, humans do what the product appears to automate.

3. Measure after shipping:

• Adoption: Are users finding and using the new feature? • Retention: Did this feature improve retention for users who adopted it? • Support: Did support tickets about this area increase or decrease? • NPS: Did overall satisfaction change after launch? • Revenue: Did this feature drive upgrades or expansion? • Timebox: Set a 30-60-90 day review for every major feature launch.

User Research Checklist

☐ Research plan defined (goals, methods, frequency, budget) ☐ Research calendar created (interviews, surveys, usability tests scheduled) ☐ Participant recruitment process defined (customers, prospects, churned users) ☐ Interview guide template created (open-ended questions, follow-up prompts) ☐ Survey templates designed (NPS, CSAT, PMF, feature requests) ☐ Usability testing process established (tasks, metrics, tools) ☐ Behavioral analytics configured (funnels, cohorts, session recordings) ☐ Support ticket categorization implemented (top issues tracked) ☐ Sales feedback loop created (objections, wins/losses shared with product) ☐ Churn analysis process defined (exit surveys, win-back interviews) ☐ Research repository built (centralized storage of insights, tagged, searchable) ☐ Insight sharing process established (weekly research debriefs) ☐ Product team trained on research methods (interviewing, survey design, analysis) ☐ Incentive budget allocated ($50-100 per interview participant) ☐ Tools selected and configured (Typeform, UserTesting, Hotjar, Calendly) ☐ Research velocity target set (5 interviews/month, 1 survey/quarter) ☐ Personas documented (based on research, not assumptions) ☐ Jobs-to-be-Done framework applied (what job are customers hiring your product for?) ☐ Competitive research ongoing (what alternatives do customers consider?) ☐ Market research conducted (TAM, SAM, SOM analysis) ☐ User journey mapped (all touchpoints, pain points, emotions) ☐ Feedback widget installed in product (in-app NPS, feature requests, bug reports) ☐ Beta tester community established (early access for research participants) ☐ Research ethics considered (informed consent, data privacy, vulnerable populations) ☐ Diversity in research participants (different roles, company sizes, industries, geographies) ☐ Research ROI tracked (features built from research, churn prevented, revenue impact) ☐ Continuous research culture established (research is everyone's job, not just researchers)
