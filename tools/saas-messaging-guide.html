✍️ Messaging

Write copy that converts.

What is Messaging?

Messaging is the words you use to communicate your product's value. It's not just marketing copy — it's every piece of text a user sees: homepage headlines, button labels, error messages, onboarding slides, email subject lines, pricing descriptions, and support responses.

Great messaging does three things: it captures attention, communicates value, and drives action. Bad messaging does the opposite: it confuses, it bores, or it overwhelms.

Messaging is not positioning. Positioning is the strategy of where you sit in the market. Messaging is the execution of that strategy in words. You can have great positioning and terrible messaging (or vice versa). Both need to work together.

Messaging Principles

1. Lead with outcomes, not features:

Bad: "Our platform has real-time collaboration, version control, and 200+ integrations."
Good: "Ship products 3x faster with your team working in one place."

Users don't buy features. They buy better versions of themselves. "What will my life look like after using this product?" That's what messaging should answer.

2. Speak your customer's language:

Use the words your customers use, not the words your engineering team uses. If customers say "I need to keep track of my leads," don't say "Our CRM provides lead management functionality." Say "Never lose a lead again."

3. Be specific:

Specificity beats superlatives. "Save 10 hours a week on reporting" beats "The best reporting tool." "Used by 500+ startups" beats "Trusted by industry leaders." Numbers and specifics create credibility.

4. Use the "So what?" test:

For every claim, ask "So what?" until you reach the real benefit.

"We use AI." → So what? → "You get insights without writing SQL." → So what? → "You make decisions in minutes instead of days."

The final answer is your message.

5. Match the moment:

Different stages of the user journey need different messaging: • Awareness: Problem-focused. "Tired of [pain]?" • Consideration: Solution-focused. "Here's how [product] solves it." • Decision: Proof-focused. "Join 10,000+ companies who switched." • Onboarding: Action-focused. "Connect your first integration in 2 minutes." • Retention: Value-focused. "You saved 5 hours this week."

6. Write for scanning:

Users don't read, they scan. Make your messaging scannable: • Short paragraphs (2-3 lines max) • Bullet lists for features and benefits • Bold text for key phrases • Headings that tell the story • White space between sections • One idea per section

7. Avoid jargon:

"Leverage our AI-powered synergistic platform to optimize your workflow." Nobody talks like this. Write like you're explaining to a smart friend at dinner. If you wouldn't say it out loud, don't write it.

8. Test everything:

Headlines, CTAs, email subjects, button text — all of it should be A/B tested. The words you think will work often don't. "Get Started Free" might beat "Start Your Free Trial" by 15%. You won't know without testing.

Messaging by Channel

🏠 Homepage
The homepage has 3 jobs: communicate what you do, who it's for, and why it's different. • Headline (10 words max): The ultimate outcome for your target customer. • Subheadline (20-30 words): How you achieve that outcome, with a hint at differentiation. • CTA: One primary action. "Start Free Trial" or "Get Started" or "See a Demo." • Social proof: Logos, testimonials, or stats that build trust immediately below the fold. Example: Headline: "The fastest way to build internal tools" Subheadline: "Connect your database, build CRUD apps, and deploy in minutes — no frontend code needed." CTA: "Start Building Free →"
📧 Email
Email is personal. Write like a human. • Subject line: Curiosity, urgency, or specific benefit. 40-50 characters. No clickbait. • Preview text: The second subject line. Use it to add context, not repeat the subject. • Opening: Get to the point in the first sentence. "You asked for X. Here it is." • Body: One goal per email. Don't ask users to read, click, reply, and share in the same message. • CTA: One button, clear action. "Complete Setup" not "Click Here." • Signature: A real person's name, not "The Team." Humans send emails to humans. Example subject: "Your free trial ends tomorrow — here's how to keep your data"
💰 Pricing Page
Pricing pages sell value, not features. • Plan names: Use outcome-based names. "Starter", "Growth", "Scale" beats "Basic", "Pro", "Enterprise." • Plan descriptions: One sentence on who it's for. "For teams just getting started" vs. "For scaling companies with complex needs." • Feature bullets: Lead with the most valuable features. Use checkmarks for included, grayed-out for not included. • Social proof near pricing: "Join 10,000+ companies" or "Most popular plan" badge on the middle tier. • FAQ: Address objections directly. "Can I change plans?" "What's the refund policy?" • CTA: "Start Free Trial" on each plan. Make it easy to choose.
🎓 Onboarding
Onboarding messaging reduces friction and builds confidence. • Welcome: "You're 2 minutes away from [outcome]." Set expectations. • Progress: "Step 1 of 3" or "50% complete." Show progress to reduce abandonment. • Tooltips: Contextual help. "Click here to connect your first integration" not "This button connects integrations." • Empty states: Don't say "No data yet." Say "Connect your calendar to see your schedule here." • Error messages: Explain what happened and how to fix it. "Invalid email" → "Please enter a valid email like name@company.com." • Success states: Celebrate milestones. "🎉 You sent your first campaign! Here's what happens next."
📱 In-App
In-app messaging is context-rich. Use it wisely. • Notifications: Action-oriented. "Your report is ready" with "View Report" button. • Banners: One message at a time. Don't stack 3 banners. • Modals: Use sparingly. Only for critical actions or announcements. • Tooltips: Explain new features when users encounter them. "New: You can now bulk edit. Try it →" • Upgrade prompts: Tie to value, not restriction. "Upgrade to send unlimited emails" not "You've hit your limit."

Messaging Voice and Tone

Voice: Who you are. Consistent across all communication.

Examples:
• Slack: Friendly, playful, human. "Slack is your digital HQ." • Stripe: Technical, precise, trustworthy. "Payments infrastructure for the internet." • Notion: Clean, minimal, empowering. "Just a thought." • Mailchimp: Quirky, fun, approachable. "Email marketing for everyone."

Tone: How you sound in a specific moment. Adapts to context.

• Onboarding: Encouraging, guiding. "Let's get you set up." • Error: Calm, helpful. "Something went wrong. Here's how to fix it." • Success: Celebratory. "You did it! 🎉" • Billing: Clear, serious. "Your invoice for $49 is ready." • Churn: Empathetic, open. "We're sorry to see you go. What could we have done better?"

Document your voice and tone. Share it with the team. Every person who writes for your product should sound like the same brand.

Messaging Checklist

☐ Every message leads with the user outcome ☐ Customer language used, not internal jargon ☐ Specific numbers and details included ☐ "So what?" test passed for every claim ☐ Messaging matches the user journey stage ☐ Copy is scannable (short paragraphs, bullets, headings) ☐ No jargon or buzzwords ☐ One CTA per message/channel ☐ Voice and tone documented and shared ☐ Homepage headline under 10 words ☐ Email subject lines tested ☐ Pricing page focuses on value, not features ☐ Error messages explain and help ☐ Onboarding messaging reduces friction ☐ All messaging reviewed by someone outside the product team
