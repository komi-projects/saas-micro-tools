Predict revenue with confidence.

Forecasting Methods

1. Bottom-Up Forecasting
Start with individual deals and sum up. Most accurate but requires clean pipeline data. Best for sales-led businesses with defined stages and probabilities.
2. Top-Down Forecasting
Start with market size and take a percentage. "The market is $1B, we'll capture 0.1% = $1M." Good for investor pitches, bad for operational planning.
3. Historical Growth Rate
Apply past growth rates to current numbers. "We grew 10% monthly last year." Simple but assumes conditions stay the same. Adjust for seasonality and market changes.
4. Cohort-Based Forecasting
Forecast by customer cohort. "January cohort: 100 customers, 5% monthly churn, $100 ARPU." Most accurate for SaaS but requires good data infrastructure.

Building Your Forecast

Step 1: Baseline — Start with current MRR/ARR.

Step 2: Existing Customer Impact — Apply expected churn, expansion, and contraction.

Step 3: New Customer Impact — Forecast new ARR from marketing and sales pipeline.

Step 4: Scenario Modeling — Build best case, base case, and worst case.

Step 5: Review and Adjust — Compare forecast vs actual monthly. Improve assumptions over time.

Example: 12-Month MRR Forecast

Base Case
Starting MRR: $50,000
Monthly churn: 5%
Monthly new MRR: $8,000
Monthly expansion: $2,000

Month 1: $50K - $2.5K (churn) + $8K (new) + $2K (expansion) = $57.5K
Month 6: ~$85K (40% growth in 6 months)
Month 12: ~$125K (150% growth in 12 months)

Annual ARR: $50K → $125K = 2.5x growth

Forecast Accuracy Benchmarks

• 1-month forecast: ± 5% is excellent
• 3-month forecast: ± 10% is good
• 12-month forecast: ± 20% is realistic

If your forecasts are consistently wrong: Your assumptions are broken, not your math. Common culprits: overstated win rates, underestimated churn, overly optimistic new customer acquisition.

Cash Flow vs Revenue Forecasting

Revenue forecasting: Predicts recognized revenue (MRR/ARR). For investor reporting and strategic planning.

Cash flow forecasting: Predicts actual cash in the bank. For operational decisions and runway planning.

Key difference: Annual contracts show all cash upfront but recognize revenue monthly. A $12K annual deal = $12K cash in month 1, but only $1K MRR.

Rule: Always forecast both. Revenue tells you if the business model works. Cash tells you if you'll make payroll.
