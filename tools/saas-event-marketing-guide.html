🎪 Event Marketing

Use events to generate leads and accelerate pipeline.

Why Events for SaaS

Events are the highest-ROI channel in B2B SaaS. A single conversation at a conference can close a $50K deal that would have taken 6 months of cold outreach. Events compress the sales cycle because prospects can see your product, meet your team, and build trust in hours instead of months.

But events are expensive. A conference booth can cost $10K-$100K when you include travel, staff, and materials. The key is being strategic — choosing the right events, setting clear goals, and measuring actual pipeline impact, not just "brand awareness."

This guide covers how to use events to generate leads, build relationships, and drive revenue for your SaaS.

Event Types

1. Industry Conferences
Large trade shows where your target customers gather. • Example: SaaStr, Web Summit, Collision, industry-specific shows • Cost: $5K-$100K+ (booth, sponsorship, travel, staff) • Audience: Mixed (decision makers, practitioners, vendors, students) • Goal: Brand awareness, lead generation, partnership meetings • Best for: Established SaaS with budget and sales team
2. Hosted Events (Your Own)
Dinners, workshops, or meetups you organize. • Example: "SaaS Founders Dinner" for 20 prospects in a city • Cost: $1K-$10K (venue, food, materials) • Audience: Curated list of target accounts • Goal: Deep relationships, account-based marketing, customer intimacy • Best for: Enterprise sales, relationship-driven deals
3. Virtual Events and Webinars
Online events with global reach and lower cost. • Example: Monthly webinar series on industry topics • Cost: $500-$5K (platform, promotion, speaker fees) • Audience: Broad, global, lower commitment than in-person • Goal: Lead generation, thought leadership, content repurposing • Best for: All stages, especially early-stage with limited budget
4. Speaking Engagements
Your founder or team member presents at someone else's event. • Example: Keynote at SaaStr, panel at industry meetup • Cost: Often free (or paid if you're a headliner) • Audience: Self-selected (people interested in your topic) • Goal: Thought leadership, lead generation, brand credibility • Best for: Founders with expertise and stage presence
5. Community Meetups
Small, regular gatherings organized by or for your community. • Example: Monthly "SaaS Growth Meetup" in major cities • Cost: $200-$2K (venue, drinks, name tags) • Audience: Highly engaged, local community members • Goal: Community building, customer intimacy, local awareness • Best for: Community-led growth, local market penetration

Event Strategy

1. Choose events strategically:

• Where are your target customers? If you sell to fintech, go to fintech conferences, not general SaaS shows. • Who attended last year? Request attendee lists (or analyze social media for past attendees). • What's the buyer mix? 80% vendors and 20% buyers is a bad ratio. Look for buyer-heavy events. • What's the stage fit? A 3-person startup at a $50K booth at Dreamforce is probably wasteful. • What's the competitive presence? If all your competitors are there, you probably should be too. • Cost per qualified lead: Track this. Some expensive events deliver cheap leads. Some cheap events deliver no leads.

2. Set clear goals:

• Lead generation: "Capture 100 qualified leads" • Pipeline: "Generate $500K in influenced pipeline" • Brand awareness: "Reach 1,000 target accounts" • Customer meetings: "Meet face-to-face with 10 enterprise accounts" • Partnerships: "Meet with 5 potential integration partners" • Recruiting: "Talk to 20 potential engineering candidates" • Content: "Record 5 customer interviews for case studies" • Pick 1-2 primary goals. Events fail when you try to do everything.

3. Pre-event preparation:

• Prospect list: Identify target accounts attending. Book meetings in advance. • Outreach: Email prospects 2 weeks before. "We'll be at [event]. Want to grab coffee?" • Content: Prepare demos, presentations, and one-pagers specific to the event audience. • Staff training: Everyone at the booth should know the pitch, the product, and the goals. • Lead capture: QR codes, badge scanners, or simple signup forms. Don't rely on business cards. • Swag: Useful, not junk. Notebooks, chargers, socks. Things people keep and use. • Calendar: Block time for booth duty, meetings, meals, and rest. Events are marathons.

At-Event Execution

1. Booth strategy:

• Location matters: Near entrances, food, or main stage. Avoid the back corner. • Design for conversation: Open layout, seating, demo stations. Not a fortress behind a table. • Demo loop: 2-minute demos on repeat. Don't make people sit through 20 minutes. • Lead qualification: Ask 2 quick questions to qualify. Don't collect every passerby's badge. • Staff rotation: 2-hour shifts max. Fresh energy converts better. • Follow-up on-site: If someone is hot, book a meeting for tomorrow. Don't wait until you're back home.

2. Networking:

• Don't hide in the booth. Walk the floor. Attend sessions. Go to parties. • Quality over quantity: 5 meaningful conversations beat 50 badge scans. • Be helpful, not salesy: "What brings you to the event?" opens more doors than "Want a demo?" • Take notes: After each conversation, jot down context. You'll meet 50 people. You'll forget 45. • Connect on LinkedIn immediately: Before the event ends, while they remember you. • Host a dinner: Invite 8-10 prospects to dinner. Intimate, memorable, expensive but effective.

Post-Event Follow-Up

1. Speed matters:

• Follow up within 24 hours while the event is fresh in their memory. • Reference the conversation: "Great chatting about [topic] at [event]." • Provide value: Share a resource related to what you discussed. Not just "thanks for visiting our booth." • Clear CTA: "Want to see a personalized demo?" or "Here's that case study I mentioned." • Cadence: First follow-up within 24h. Second at 1 week. Third at 2 weeks if no response. • CRM entry: Log every interaction before you forget the details.

2. Internal debrief:

• What worked? (booth location, demo format, specific conversations) • What didn't? (low-quality leads, staffing issues, competitor noise) • ROI calculation: Cost / qualified leads / pipeline generated / closed revenue • Should we return next year? With what changes? • Content capture: Turn event insights into blog posts, social content, and sales enablement

Event Metrics

Leading indicators:

• Leads captured (qualified, not total) • Meetings booked (pre-event and on-site) • Demo requests • Social mentions and engagement during the event • Content pieces created (photos, videos, live tweets)

Lagging indicators:

• Pipeline generated from event leads (track in CRM) • Meetings held post-event • Trials started from event leads • Conversion rate: event leads vs. non-event leads • Revenue closed from event-sourced pipeline • Cost per qualified lead • Cost per dollar of pipeline • ROI: (Revenue - Cost) / Cost

Important: Event ROI has a long tail. A conversation in March might close in December. Track influenced pipeline, not just immediate conversions.

Event Mistakes

1. Attending without goals: "We're going because our competitors are." Without clear goals, you won't know if it was worth it.

2. Over-investing too early: A $50K booth at Dreamforce when you have 3 customers. Start with smaller events and speaking engagements.

3. Collecting junk leads: 500 badge scans, 10 qualified prospects. Focus on quality conversations, not quantity of scans.

4. No follow-up: Leads sit in a spreadsheet for 3 weeks. By then, they've forgotten you. Follow up within 24 hours.

5. Sending the wrong people: Junior SDRs who can't answer technical questions. Founders who only want to network and ignore booth duty. Send people who can both pitch and listen.

6. Ignoring the event itself: Your team sits in the booth all day and misses the sessions, the hallway conversations, and the dinners where real deals happen.

Event Checklist
