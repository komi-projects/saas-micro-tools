💼 Sales Playbook

Build repeatable sales processes that close.

Why Sales Playbooks Win

A sales playbook is the difference between a team of heroes and a team of professionals. Without a playbook, every rep reinvents the wheel. They write their own emails, create their own decks, and develop their own processes. Some get lucky. Most don't.

With a playbook, your entire team operates from the same proven system. New hires ramp in weeks, not months. Experienced reps optimize instead of inventing. Managers coach instead of救火. And your results become predictable and scalable.

In SaaS, where sales cycles are complex and buyer committees are large, playbooks are essential. They ensure consistent messaging, proper qualification, and systematic progression through each stage of the deal.

This guide covers how to build, maintain, and execute sales playbooks that turn your sales team into a revenue machine.

Sales Plays

1. Discovery Play
Understand the customer's situation before pitching. • Goal: Identify pain, budget, authority, timeline, and competitive landscape • Questions: "What led you to look for a solution now?" "How are you handling this today?" "What happens if you don't solve this?" • Listen ratio: Talk 30%, listen 70%. The best reps are investigators, not presenters. • Qualification: Use MEDDIC, BANT, or SPICED to score the opportunity • Output: Qualified opportunity with clear next steps, or disqualified early (saving everyone time)
2. Demo Play
Show value, not features. • Goal: Prove your product solves their specific pain • Preparation: Never demo without discovery. Customize the demo to their use case. • Structure: Problem → Solution → Proof → Next Steps (15-20 minutes max) • Interactive: Let them click, type, and explore. Don't just present slides. • Objection handling: Address "How does this work with...?" and "What about...?" in real-time • Close with clear next step: "Let's schedule the technical review for Thursday. Does 2pm work?"
3. Trial Close Play
Convert trial users to paying customers. • Goal: Maximize trial-to-paid conversion rate • Engagement: Check in at day 1, 3, 7, and 14. Not to sell — to help. • Milestone tracking: Are they hitting activation events? If not, intervene. • Success planning: "What does success look like in this trial? Let's make sure you achieve it." • Expansion preview: "Your team has 5 people using it. The Team plan includes admin controls." • Urgency: Trial expiration is natural urgency. Don't manufacture fake deadlines.
4. Competitive Displacement Play
Win deals against incumbent competitors. • Goal: Position your solution as clearly superior for their specific needs • Research: Know the competitor's weaknesses. Read their reviews, talk to their ex-customers. • Don't trash competitors: "Many teams outgrow [Competitor] when they need [capability]. Let me show you how we handle that." • ROI focus: "You're paying $X for Y seats. Here's how we deliver more value at a lower total cost." • Risk mitigation: "We'll handle the migration. Your data will be transferred securely." • Reference: "[Similar Company] switched from [Competitor] 6 months ago. Want to chat with their VP?"
5. Expansion Play
Grow revenue within existing accounts. • Goal: Increase ACV through upgrades, add-ons, and seat expansion • Signals: Usage approaching limits, team growth, new use cases, feature requests • Timing: Reach out 60-90 days before renewal, not at expiration • Value demonstration: "Your team has grown 40%. Here's how the Enterprise plan supports that scale." • Multi-threading: Engage new stakeholders (IT, Finance, new team leads) • Success stories: "[Similar Company] upgraded last quarter and saw [result]."
6. Renewal Play
Secure renewals with expansion if possible. • Goal: 100% gross retention + net expansion • Early start: Begin renewal conversations 90 days before expiration • Health check: "How has your experience been? What's working? What's not?" • Value recap: "In the past year, you've [accomplished X]. Here's your ROI summary." • Expansion pitch: "Based on your growth, the Pro plan would save you $X and add [features]." • Risk mitigation: If health scores are low, create a recovery plan before renewal • Multi-year option: "Would you like to lock in this rate for 2 years?"

Building Your Playbook

1. Document your sales process:

• Stages: Define each stage from lead to close (e.g., Qualification → Demo → Trial → Negotiation → Close) • Exit criteria: What must happen to move a deal from one stage to the next? • Activities: What actions does the rep take at each stage? (calls, emails, demos, proposals) • Duration: How long should each stage take? (alerts if deals stall) • Conversion rates: Historical % of deals that advance from each stage • Tools: Which tools are used at each stage? (CRM, demo software, proposal tool, e-signature)

2. Create sales assets:

• Email templates: Outreach, follow-up, reminder, and breakup sequences for each persona • Call scripts: Discovery questions, demo talking points, objection responses • Decks: Standard pitch deck, vertical-specific decks, competitive comparison slides • Demo environment: Pre-configured demo account with realistic data • Proposal templates: Standard pricing, terms, and contract language • Battlecards: One-pagers on each competitor (strengths, weaknesses, positioning, objection handling) • ROI calculator: Spreadsheet or web tool showing customer-specific return

3. Define qualification criteria:

• MEDDIC: Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Identify Pain, Champion • BANT: Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline • SPICED: Situation, Problem, Implication, Critical Event, Decision • Custom: Build your own based on your market and sales cycle • Disqualification: Define when to walk away. Bad deals waste time and hurt morale. • Scoring: Rate opportunities 1-100. Focus on 70+ scores. Nurture or disqualify the rest.

4. Set targets and metrics:

• Activity targets: Calls, emails, demos per day/week • Pipeline targets: Qualified opportunities, pipeline value per rep • Conversion targets: Stage-to-stage advancement rates • Revenue targets: Quota per rep, per team, per quarter • Quality targets: Average deal size, sales cycle length, win rate

Sales Enablement

1. Training:

• Onboarding: New hires complete playbook certification before taking calls • Shadowing: New reps shadow top performers for 2 weeks • Role-play: Practice discovery calls, demos, and objection handling weekly • Certifications: Quarterly assessments on product knowledge, competitive positioning, and process • Continuous learning: Share wins, losses, and market insights in weekly team meetings

2. Coaching:

• Call reviews: Listen to recordings together. What went well? What could improve? • Pipeline reviews: Weekly 1:1s reviewing each rep's pipeline. Focus on stalled deals and next steps. • Data-driven: Use CRM data to identify patterns. "Your demo-to-trial rate is 20% below average. Let's fix that." • Peer coaching: Top reps mentor newer ones. Creates camaraderie and spreads best practices. • Self-coaching: Reps listen to their own calls and score themselves against the playbook.

3. Content:

• Playbook wiki: Centralized, searchable, always up-to-date (Notion, Confluence, Guru) • Battlecards: Competitor intel updated monthly by product marketing • Case studies: Sales-ready stories with metrics, quotes, and use cases • Objection library: "When they say X, say Y." Collected from real calls. • Email templates: A/B tested sequences that convert. Updated based on performance data. • Product updates: Weekly digest of new features, positioning, and talking points

Sales Metrics

Activity:

• Calls made, emails sent, demos delivered per rep per week • Response rates: % of outreach that generates a reply • Meeting conversion: % of conversations that turn into meetings • Pipeline generation: New opportunities created per month

Pipeline:>/p>

• Pipeline coverage: Total pipeline / quota (benchmark: 3-4x) • Stage conversion: % of deals advancing from each stage • Average deal size: Total revenue / closed deals • Sales cycle length: Days from first touch to close • Win rate: % of qualified opportunities that close • Deal velocity: (Win rate × Average deal size) / Sales cycle length

Revenue:

• Quota attainment: % of reps hitting quota • Revenue per rep: ARR generated per sales head • CAC payback: Months to recover customer acquisition cost • LTV:CAC ratio: Lifetime value vs. acquisition cost (benchmark: 3:1+) • Expansion rate: % of revenue from existing account growth • Churn rate: % of revenue lost to cancellations

Sales Playbook Mistakes

1. Over-engineering: A 200-page playbook nobody reads. Keep it concise, visual, and searchable. Reps need quick answers, not a novel.

2. Set and forget: Playbooks written once and never updated. Markets change, competitors evolve, products improve. Review quarterly.

3. One-size-fits-all: The same playbook for enterprise and SMB, for all verticals. Segment your plays. A founder needs different messaging than an IT director.

4. No enforcement: Playbook exists but reps ignore it. Make playbook adherence part of coaching and reviews. Celebrate reps who use it.

5. Feature-focused demos: Reps show every feature instead of solving the customer's problem. Train on value-based selling, not feature tours.

6. Ignoring losses: Wins celebrated, losses ignored. The best learning comes from losses. Conduct win/loss interviews and share insights.

Sales Playbook Checklist

☐ Sales stages defined with exit criteria ☐ Discovery questions documented by persona ☐ Demo script and environment prepared ☐ Email templates for each stage (A/B tested) ☐ Call scripts for common scenarios ☐ Objection handling guide with real responses ☐ Competitive battlecards for top 5 competitors ☐ ROI calculator or value justification tool ☐ Proposal and contract templates ☐ Qualification framework adopted (MEDDIC/BANT/SPICED) ☐ Sales targets and KPIs defined ☐ CRM configured with stages, fields, and automation ☐ Rep onboarding program with certification ☐ Weekly coaching cadence established ☐ Win/loss interview process defined ☐ Playbook wiki live and accessible ☐ Quarterly playbook review scheduled ☐ Sales and marketing alignment on messaging and ICP
