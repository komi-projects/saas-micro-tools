📊 409A Valuation Guide

When you need one, how much it costs, and how to minimize it.

What is a 409A Valuation?

A 409A valuation determines the fair market value (FMV) of your company's common stock. The IRS requires it so employees don't get taxed on "cheap" stock options. If your strike price is below FMV, it's a taxable event.

Bottom line: You need a 409A to legally grant stock options.

When Do You Need One?

🟢 Before first stock option grant
Legally required. No 409A = illegal options = tax nightmare for employees.
🟡 After any material event
New funding round, major customer win, product launch that changes valuation. "Material" = anything that would change FMV by > 10%.
🟡 Annually (minimum)
Even if nothing changed, refresh every 12 months. Courts and auditors expect this.
🔴 After IPO/acquisition discussions
Valuation changes significantly. Need fresh 409A before any new grants.

How to Minimize 409A Valuation

Lower 409A = lower strike price = more attractive for employees. Here's how to keep it down legally:

1. Time it right. Get 409A before fundraising closes. Post-money valuation = higher 409A.

2. Highlight discounts. Common stock is worth less than preferred (usually 20-40% discount). Make sure the appraiser applies this.

3. Show risk factors. Single product, limited customers, key person dependency = lower valuation.

4. Don't overstate financials. Be honest about burn rate, runway, and revenue concentration.

5. Use the right method. Early stage: OPM (Option Pricing Method). Later: PWERM (Probability Weighted Expected Return).

⚠️ Never DIY a 409A. IRS requires independent appraisal. Using a calculator or spreadsheet won't hold up in audit. The "safe harbor" provision only applies to qualified, independent appraisers.

The 409A Process

1. Gather docs: Cap table, financials, business plan, term sheets, prior valuations
2. Select appraiser: Choose based on stage and budget
3. Questionnaire: 30-60 min call about business, market, competitors
4. Draft report: Appraiser sends draft for review (2-5 days)
5. Final report: Signed, dated, ready for board approval
6. Board approval: Formal approval at next board meeting
