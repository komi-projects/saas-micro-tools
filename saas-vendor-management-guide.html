🤝 Vendor Management

Trust but verify. Diversify. Negotiate.

Why Vendor Management Matters

Modern SaaS companies depend on dozens of vendors: cloud providers, payment processors, email services, analytics tools, CRMs, support platforms, and more. When a critical vendor fails, your service fails too.

Vendor management isn't procurement bureaucracy. It's operational resilience. The 2021 Log4j vulnerability affected thousands of vendors simultaneously. Companies with vendor risk management programs responded faster. Those without scrambled for days.

Vendor Categories

🔴 Critical (Service-Impacting)
Vendors whose failure directly impacts your service. Cloud provider, database, payment processor, primary CDN. If they're down, you're down. Require: SLA with financial penalties, DR plan review, security audit, alternate vendor identified.
🟡 Important (Business-Impacting)
Vendors whose failure impacts operations but not core service. Email service, CRM, analytics, support platform. If they're down, your team can't work efficiently. Require: SLA, regular performance review, exit plan.
🟢 Standard (Convenience)
Vendors that improve efficiency but aren't essential. Design tools, project management, internal chat. If they're down, workarounds exist. Require: Basic terms review, annual cost evaluation.

Vendor Evaluation Checklist

Before signing with any critical vendor:

Technical:

• Uptime SLA: 99.9% minimum for critical, 99.99% preferred. What are the penalties? • Security: SOC 2 Type II? ISO 27001? Penetration testing results? • Compliance: Do they meet your regulatory requirements (GDPR, HIPAA)? • Performance: Latency, throughput, capacity limits. Load test if possible. • Integration: API quality, documentation, SDKs, webhook reliability. • Data portability: Can you export your data? In what format? Is there a cost?

Business:

• Financial health: Is the vendor profitable? Recent funding? Stability? • Customer concentration: Do they depend on one or two big customers? Risk if those leave. • Roadmap alignment: Does their product direction match your needs? • Support quality: Response time SLA? Dedicated account manager? 24/7 support? • Pricing: Transparent? Predictable? Lock-in risks (price increases, forced upgrades)?

Risk:

• Single point of failure: Are they the only vendor in this category? • Geographic concentration: Are their data centers all in one region? • Dependency chain: Who do they depend on? (Your vendor's vendor is your vendor) • Exit barriers: How hard is it to switch? Data lock-in? Custom integration costs? • Insurance: Do they carry cyber liability? What's their liability cap?

Contract Negotiation

Don't just sign the standard terms. Negotiate:

• SLA with teeth: Monthly uptime target, measurement methodology, credit calculation, and escalation path. 99.9% = 43 minutes downtime/month. Penalties should be meaningful (not just 10% credit). • Liability cap: Standard is 12 months of fees. For critical vendors, negotiate higher or uncapped for gross negligence. • Data ownership: You own your data, always. They can't use it for AI training, analytics, or marketing without explicit consent. • Termination: 30-90 day termination for convenience. Data export assistance. No penalty for leaving. • Price protection: Lock pricing for 1-2 years. Cap annual increases at 5-10%. • Auto-renewal: Opt-in, not opt-out. 60-day notice before renewal. • Security requirements: MFA for their admin access to your account. Annual security questionnaires. Breach notification within 24 hours.

Vendor Diversification

Don't put all eggs in one basket:

• Payment processors: Primary (Stripe), backup (PayPal, Adyen). If one goes down, route to another. • Cloud regions: Deploy across multiple regions. Use DNS failover or load balancer health checks. • Email providers: Primary (SendGrid), backup (AWS SES). Switch via configuration, not code changes. • CDNs: Multi-CDN (Cloudflare + Fastly). Some use DNS-based failover, others use client-side switching. • Databases: Read replicas across providers for DR. Don't make your primary DB the only copy.

When NOT to diversify: Small scale (< $1M ARR) — operational complexity outweighs benefits. Stick with best-in-class single vendors and focus on product-market fit.

Vendor Performance Monitoring

Track vendor performance like you track your own:

• Uptime tracking: Monitor their status page. Log incidents. Compare actual uptime to SLA. • Latency monitoring: Track API response times. Alert if P95 exceeds threshold. • Error rate: Log vendor errors (5xx, timeouts, rate limits). Alert if error rate > 1%. • Quarterly business reviews (QBR): For critical vendors. Review performance, roadmap, issues, and contract terms. • Annual evaluation: Scorecard: performance, support, roadmap, price, relationship. Decide: renew, renegotiate, or replace.

Vendor Exit Strategy

Every vendor relationship should have a planned ending:

• Data export: Regular automated exports of critical data. Don't wait until you're leaving to figure out data extraction. • Alternative vendor: Identified, evaluated, and ready to activate. Know pricing, integration effort, and migration timeline. • Integration abstraction: Wrap vendor APIs in your own interfaces. Switching vendors shouldn't require rewriting your entire application. • Contract terms: Ensure you can leave without penalties. Month-to-month preferred for non-critical vendors. • Transition timeline: Plan for 1-3 months to migrate critical vendors. Parallel run during transition (both old and new active).
