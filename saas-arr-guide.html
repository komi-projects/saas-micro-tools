What is ARR?

Annual Recurring Revenue is your MRR multiplied by 12. It's the standard metric investors and acquirers use to value SaaS companies. ARR represents the predictable, recurring revenue you expect over a year.

ARR = MRR × 12

Why ARR matters: Investors think in ARR. Valuations are quoted as ARR multiples. A company with $1M ARR might be valued at 10x ARR = $10M.

ARR Valuation Multiples

SaaS valuations are typically quoted as multiples of ARR:

Valuation Ranges
• Low growth (< 20% YoY): 2-4x ARR
• Moderate growth (20-50% YoY): 4-8x ARR
• High growth (50-100% YoY): 8-15x ARR
• Hyper growth (> 100% YoY): 15-25x+ ARR

Example: $5M ARR growing 80% YoY with 120% NRR = ~$50-75M valuation

ARR Growth Benchmarks

• $0-$1M ARR: 3x YoY growth (triple every year)
• $1M-$10M ARR: 2x YoY growth (double every year)
• $10M-$50M ARR: 50% YoY growth
• $50M+ ARR: 30% YoY growth

Rule of 40: Growth rate + Profit margin ≥ 40%. A company growing 60% with -20% margin hits the Rule of 40.

ARR vs Revenue

ARR ≠ Total Revenue. ARR is only the recurring portion:

• ARR: Subscription revenue that recurs annually
• Total Revenue: ARR + one-time fees + services + non-recurring

Example: A company with $2M ARR might have $2.5M total revenue including $500K in professional services.

Common Mistakes

❌ Using bookings instead of recognized revenue. A $12K annual contract signed in December contributes $1K to December MRR, not $12K.

❌ Including monthly subscriptions as ARR. A $100/mo subscription = $1,200 ARR, not $100.

❌ Ignoring churn in projections. If you have $1M ARR and 10% annual churn, you need $100K in new ARR just to stay flat.
