What is a Viral Loop?

A viral loop is when using your product naturally leads to inviting new users. The classic example: you send a Dropbox link to a friend. They need Dropbox to view it. They sign up. Now they send links to their friends.

The formula: Every user brings in > 1 new user = exponential growth. Every user brings in < 1 new user = linear growth (or death).

The Viral Coefficient (K-Factor)

K = i × c

i = invites per user
c = conversion rate of invites (% who sign up)

• K < 1: Virality helps but isn't self-sustaining
• K = 1: Linear growth (every user replaces themselves)
• K > 1: Exponential growth (true viral product)

Reality check: Most "viral" SaaS has K = 0.2-0.5. K > 1 is rare (WhatsApp, Zoom early days). Don't expect true virality — even K = 0.3 reduces CAC by 30%.

4 Types of Viral Loops

1. Collaboration Loop (Slack, Notion, Figma)
User invites teammates to collaborate. The more people join, the more valuable the product becomes for everyone. Network effect compounds.
2. Communication Loop (Loom, Calendly, Typeform)
User shares content created with your product. Recipients see the product in action before signing up. Every share = free ad.
3. Referral Loop (Dropbox, Uber, PayPal)
Explicit incentive: "Invite a friend, get free storage/credit." Works best when the reward is the product itself (not cash).
4. Exposure Loop (Grammarly, Wise, Linktree)
Product is visible in public outputs. Grammarly corrections show the brand. Wise payment links expose the service. Every use = brand impression.

Examples That Work

📹 Loom — Communication Loop
Every Loom video shared = recipient sees Loom player. No account needed to watch, but watermark and "Get Loom" CTA drive signups. K-factor ≈ 0.4.
📅 Calendly — Utility Loop
Calendly links are shared with every meeting invite. Recipients book time without signing up, but see the product. Power users (recipients) become new users. K-factor ≈ 0.3.
📁 Dropbox — Referral Loop
"Invite a friend, get 500MB free." Both sides get reward. Dropbox grew from 100K to 4M users in 15 months with this single mechanic. K-factor peaked at > 1.0.

Designing Your Viral Loop

1. Identify the natural share moment. When does a user need to involve someone else? That's your loop.

2. Reduce friction for the recipient. Don't require signup to see value. Dropbox lets you view files without an account. Loom lets you watch videos without signing up.

3. Create incentive symmetry. Both inviter and invitee should benefit. Dropbox: both get storage. Uber: both get ride credit.

4. Make it feel native, not spammy. The invite should feel like a natural part of using the product, not a separate "refer a friend" page.

5. Track and optimize. Measure invites per user, invite conversion rate, and viral contribution to new signups. A/B test invite copy, timing, and incentives.
