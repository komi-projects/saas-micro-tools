🔬 User Research

Understand your users and build products they love.

Why User Research is Non-Negotiable

User research is the foundation of every successful SaaS product. Without it, you're building based on assumptions — and assumptions are expensive. Research reduces risk, validates ideas before you build, and ensures you're solving real problems for real people. The best SaaS companies don't just collect feedback; they systematically study their users' behaviors, motivations, and pain points.

Research isn't a one-time event; it's an ongoing practice. Early-stage companies use research to find product-market fit. Growth-stage companies use it to optimize conversion and retention. Enterprise companies use it to understand complex workflows and buying committees. At every stage, research separates products that survive from products that thrive.

This guide covers how to conduct effective user research — from interviews and usability tests to surveys and analytics synthesis — so you can build with confidence.

Research Methods

1. User Interviews
Deep qualitative insights from structured conversations. • 1-on-1 interviews: 30-60 minutes, semi-structured script • Group interviews: 3-5 users with similar backgrounds • Contextual inquiry: Observe users in their actual work environment • Jobs-to-be-Done: Focus on the "job" the user hires your product to do • Discovery vs. validation: Early interviews explore problems; later ones test solutions • Sample size: 5-8 interviews per segment for pattern detection
2. Usability Testing
Watch users interact with your product and identify friction. • Moderated: Researcher guides user through tasks, asks questions • Unmoderated: User completes tasks on their own (UserTesting, Maze) • Think-aloud: User verbalizes their thoughts while using the product • Task-based: "Show me how you would [complete specific task]" • First-click testing: Where do users click first? (predicts success) • 5-second test: What do users remember after 5 seconds on a page?
3. Surveys
Quantify attitudes, behaviors, and preferences at scale. • NPS: Net Promoter Score (likelihood to recommend) • CSAT: Customer Satisfaction Score (satisfaction with specific interaction) • CES: Customer Effort Score (ease of completing a task) • Product/market fit survey: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" • Needs assessment: Prioritize feature requests across your user base • Demographics/firmographics: Segment users by role, industry, company size
4. Analytics Synthesis
Use behavioral data to understand what users actually do. • Funnel analysis: Where do users drop off in key workflows? • Heatmaps: Where do users click, scroll, and spend time? (Hotjar, Crazy Egg) • Session recordings: Watch real user sessions (anonymized) • Cohort analysis: How do different user groups behave over time? • A/B testing: Compare user behavior between variants • Correlation analysis: Which behaviors predict retention, expansion, or churn?
5. Competitive Research
Understand how users solve their problems today. • Switch interviews: Why did users switch to/from competitors? • Competitive teardown: Analyze competitor UX, features, pricing, messaging • Review mining: Analyze G2, Capterra, TrustRadius reviews for pain points • Win/loss analysis: Why did you win or lose specific deals? • Jobs-to-be-Done: What "job" do users currently hire competitors for? • Market mapping: Position competitors on price, features, target segment

Research Process

1. Define your research goal:

• What decision will this research inform? • What do you need to know? What do you already know? • Who is the target user for this research? • What's the timeline? (Research should fit the decision cadence) • Example: "We need to understand why 60% of trial users don't activate within 7 days"

2. Choose your method:

• Exploratory (we don't know the problem): Interviews, ethnographic research • Generative (we know the problem, need solutions): Brainstorming sessions, co-design workshops • Evaluative (we have a solution, need validation): Usability tests, prototype tests • Quantitative (we need to measure): Surveys, A/B tests, analytics

3. Recruit participants:

• Current customers: Most accessible, but may be biased toward current product • Trial users: Great for understanding activation friction • Churned users: Honest about why they left • Non-users: Understand the problem space before they're influenced by your solution • Incentives: $25-100 for 30-60 minute interviews, gift cards, account credit • Screening: Ensure participants match your target persona

4. Conduct research:

• Interviews: Use a script but be flexible. Follow interesting threads. • Ask open-ended questions: "Tell me about..." not "Do you like..." • Avoid leading questions: "How frustrating was that?" assumes it was frustrating • Listen more than you talk: 80/20 rule — user talks 80% of the time • Record (with permission): You can't capture everything in notes • Take a partner: One person interviews, the other takes notes

5. Analyze and synthesize:

• Transcribe interviews or use AI transcription tools • Tag observations by theme: Pain points, needs, behaviors, quotes • Look for patterns: What came up in 3+ interviews? That's significant. • Create personas: Archetypal users based on research findings • Journey maps: Visualize the user's end-to-end experience • Prioritize: Which insights are most actionable and highest impact?

6. Share and act:

• Research report: Key findings, quotes, recommendations, next steps • Share widely: Product, engineering, marketing, sales, support — everyone benefits • Make it visual: Journey maps, personas, and video clips are more impactful than text • Track impact: Did research findings lead to product changes? What was the result? • Build repository: Store research so future teams can build on it

Research by Stage

Pre-launch (0-10 customers):

• Problem discovery interviews: 20-30 conversations with target users • Jobs-to-be-Done research: What job are they currently hiring for? • Solution validation: Mockups, prototypes, landing page tests • Pricing research: Van Westendorp, willingness-to-pay interviews • Goal: Validate problem, solution, and business model before building

Early stage (10-100 customers):

• Product-market fit survey: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" • Activation research: Why do some users activate and others don't? • Onboarding usability: Watch new users try the product for the first time • Feature prioritization: Which features do users need most? • Goal: Find product-market fit and optimize activation

Growth stage (100-1000 customers):

• Segmentation research: How do different customer types use the product? • Conversion optimization: Usability tests on signup, trial, and upgrade flows • Churn research: Exit interviews and lost customer analysis • Expansion research: What would make users upgrade or buy more seats? • Goal: Scale efficiently by understanding and serving segments

Scale (1000+ customers):

• Enterprise research: Complex workflows, security needs, buying committees • International research: Cultural differences, localization needs • Advanced feature research: Power user workflows and edge cases • Competitive research: Why do users switch to or from competitors? • Goal: Serve diverse segments and maintain market leadership

User Research Mistakes

1. Research theater:> Conducting research to confirm what you already believe. If you only listen to confirming evidence, you're not doing research — you're performing.

2. Asking users what they want: Users are great at describing their problems, terrible at designing solutions. Observe behavior, ask about problems, but design solutions yourself.

3. Too many questions: 50-question surveys or 2-hour interviews exhaust participants and yield low-quality data. Focus on 5-8 key questions per session.

4. Ignoring non-users: Only talking to current customers gives you a biased view. Talk to churned users, trial non-converters, and people who never heard of you.

5. No synthesis: Collecting data but never analyzing or sharing findings. Raw interview notes are useless — synthesis creates actionable insights.

6. Research silos: Product does interviews, support does tickets, sales does calls — but insights never cross teams. Create a shared research repository.

7. Perfectionism: Waiting for the "perfect" research plan, script, or sample size. Good research today beats perfect research next month. Start with 5 interviews.

User Research Checklist

☐ Research goals defined for each project ☐ Research method selected (interview, survey, usability test, analytics) ☐ Target participant profile defined ☐ Recruitment process established (customers, panels, incentives) ☐ Interview script or survey drafted and tested ☐ Consent forms prepared (recording, data usage) ☐ Note-taking template created ☐ 5-8 participants recruited per segment ☐ Interviews conducted and recorded ☐ Transcriptions completed (manual or AI-assisted) ☐ Data tagged and organized by theme ☐ Patterns and insights identified ☐ Personas created or updated ☐ Journey maps created or updated ☐ Research report written with key findings and recommendations ☐ Findings shared with product, engineering, marketing, sales, support ☐ Action items assigned and tracked ☐ Research repository maintained (accessible to all teams) ☐ Quarterly research calendar planned ☐ Budget allocated for incentives and tools ☐ Team trained on research methods and facilitation
