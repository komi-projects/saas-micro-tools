Business Continuity vs. Disaster Recovery

Disaster recovery is about IT systems — restoring servers, databases, and applications. Business continuity is broader — it's about keeping the business operating during and after a disruption.

BC includes: people (who does what, where do they work), processes (how do we serve customers without our usual tools), communication (how do we talk to customers, employees, partners), and facilities (where do we work if the office is unavailable). DR is a subset of BC.

BC Plan Components

1. Governance
BC program owner (usually COO or VP Operations). BC committee with reps from engineering, support, sales, finance, HR. Annual review and update cycle. Executive sponsorship is critical — BC without leadership support fails.
2. Risk Assessment
Identify threats: natural disasters, cyberattacks, pandemics, supply chain failures, key personnel loss. Score by likelihood and impact. Focus planning on high-likelihood, high-impact risks. Update quarterly.
3. Business Impact Analysis
Identify critical business functions and their dependencies. How long can each function be down before serious damage? What resources does each function need (people, systems, data, vendors)? This drives RTO and RPO targets.
4. Recovery Strategies
How do you recover each critical function? Alternate work locations, remote work capabilities, alternate suppliers, manual workarounds, cross-training employees. The goal isn't full restoration — it's maintaining minimum viable operations.
5. Communication Plan
Who communicates what, to whom, when, and how. Customer communication (status page, email, in-app). Internal communication (Slack, phone tree, emergency contact list). Vendor communication. Media/regulator communication if needed.
6. Testing and Maintenance
Tabletop exercises (quarterly), functional tests (annually), full BC drills (annually). Update plan after each test, after each real incident, and after any major organizational change (new office, new leadership, new critical systems).

SaaS-Specific BC Considerations

Remote Work: Most SaaS companies are already distributed. Ensure all employees can work from home with: laptop, internet, VPN access, collaboration tools (Slack, Zoom, Notion), and access to critical systems. Test this quarterly — don't assume it works.

Customer Communication: During a disruption, customers need to know: what's affected, how long it will last, what they should do, and how to get help. Pre-write templates for common scenarios (outage, data breach, service degradation, planned maintenance).

Revenue Protection: If your payment processor goes down, can you still bill customers? If your sales CRM is offline, can reps still close deals? Identify revenue-critical processes and ensure they have manual fallbacks.

Vendor Dependencies: List all critical vendors (cloud provider, payment processor, email service, CDN). What's your plan if each fails? Do you have alternate vendors? Can you operate without them for 24-48 hours?

Key Personnel: What if the founder/CEO is unavailable? The CTO? The head of support? Document decision-making authority, critical passwords (in a secure vault), and key contacts. Cross-train so no single person is a bottleneck.

BC During a Pandemic/Lockdown

COVID-19 taught SaaS companies valuable lessons:

• Remote-first wins: Companies already set up for remote work transitioned smoothly. Office-centric companies struggled for months. • Communication overload: Daily all-hands, constant Slack updates. Have a communication cadence planned. Too much = noise. Too little = anxiety. • Mental health: Disruptions are stressful. Build employee support into BC — mental health resources, flexible hours, clear expectations. • Customer empathy: Your customers are disrupted too. Flexible payment terms, extended trials, and proactive support build loyalty during crises. • Opportunity: Disruptions create demand shifts. Zoom grew 30x during COVID. Be ready to pivot marketing and product to meet new needs.

BC Metrics

• Plan completion: % of required BC documentation complete. Target: 100%. • Test frequency:>/strong> Tabletop exercises per quarter, full drills per year. Target: 4 tabletops, 1 full drill. • Test findings: Number of gaps identified per test. Trending down = improving. • RTO achievement: % of tests where recovery met RTO target. Target: 100%. • Employee awareness: % of employees who know the BC plan and their role. Target: 100% of critical function owners. • Revenue during disruption: Compare revenue during test/disruption to normal. Target: > 80% for minor disruptions, > 50% for major.

BC for Different Company Sizes

Startup (1-20 employees):

• Keep it simple. One-page plan with key contacts, critical systems, and basic recovery steps. • Everyone works remote. No office dependency. • Founder has access to everything (use a password manager like 1Password or Bitwarden with emergency access). • Backup critical data to cloud (Google Drive, Dropbox) in addition to production backups. • Test: Walk through the plan as a team. 30 minutes. Quarterly.

Growth (20-100 employees):

• Formal BC plan with sections for each department. • BC coordinator (part-time role, often ops or IT). • Department-specific recovery procedures. • Communication tree with alternates. • Vendor risk assessment. • Test: Tabletop exercises quarterly, functional tests annually.

Scale (100+ employees):

• Full BC program with dedicated BC manager. • BC committee with monthly meetings. • Comprehensive risk register. • Automated DR with regular failover tests. • Employee BC training as part of onboarding. • Annual third-party BC/DR audit. • Insurance review (business interruption, cyber liability).
